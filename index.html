What is Alternative Investing?

The foundation of alternatives and why they matter

An alternative investment—also known as an "alt" or "private investment"— represent investment opportunities beyond traditional stocks, bonds, and cash. For decades, these investments were reserved for institutions and the ultra-wealthy. That's changing fast.

What Counts as an Alternative Investment?

Alternative investments cover a wide range of asset classes that work differently from traditional public stocks and bonds. Examples include:

Private equity: Investments in private companies, including buyout and growth equity

Private credit: Loans and debt investments in private companies

Real estate: Direct property investments and real estate funds

Infrastructure: Investments in essential facilities and systems

Hedge funds: Pooled investment vehicles using diverse strategies

Venture capital: Early-stage company investments

Pre-IPO companies: Late-stage private companies preparing to go public

Art and collectibles: Tangible alternative assets

Each asset class has its own risk profile, liquidity characteristics, and potential returns. Understanding which alts fit your investment goals is the first step.

Alternative investments have become a critical piece of modern portfolios. In 2023, private market assets under management hit $13.1 trillion, up 12% from the year before. Projections show this could reach $15 trillion by 2025 and exceed $18 trillion by 2027. As companies stay private longer and investors look for better returns, alts have gone from "nice to have" to "need to have" in diversified portfolios.

Who's Had Access (Until Now)

Historically, alternative investments were the exclusive domain of institutional investors—pension funds, endowments, university foundations—and ultra-high-net-worth individuals with direct access to investment managers. These investors enjoyed the benefits: potential for higher returns, portfolio diversification, and access to innovative companies years before they went public.

The barriers keeping everyone else out were real: investment minimums often ran into the millions, legal structures were complex, and information was hard to come by. For most individual investors, alts simply weren't an option.

Why Things Are Changing

The alternative investment landscape is transforming. Three big shifts are making alts accessible to more accredited investors:

Companies Are Staying Private Longer

The public markets look different than they did 30 years ago. The number of public companies has dropped by 50% since then, while the number of private companies has grown by 43%.

Here's what that means: Over 19,000 private companies in the U.S. now generate more than $100 million in revenue. Compare that to fewer than 3,000 public companies of similar size. Twenty years ago, most of these companies would have already gone public.

The result? Some of today's most innovative companies—the ones building AI, revolutionizing healthcare, or creating new technologies—are staying private and building massive value outside the public markets. If you want exposure to these companies, you need access to private markets.

Technology Is Lowering the Barriers

Platforms like Monark are using technology to solve what used to be insurmountable problems. Investment minimums that were once $1 million+ are now as low as $5,000. Complex legal processes are being streamlined through digital platforms. Information that was once opaque is now transparent and accessible.

This isn't just about making things easier—it's about fundamentally changing who can participate in private markets.

The Returns Make Sense

Private equity investments have outperformed the S&P 500 over 5, 10, 15, and 20-year periods. The average 10-year return is 17.3%, compared to 11.9% for public markets.

Beyond raw returns, alternative investments help reduce portfolio risk. Because they have low correlation with public markets, they can provide a hedge when stocks are volatile. For investors looking to reduce exposure to the ups and downs of public equity markets, alts offer a real solution.

Regulations Have Evolved

Changes to accredited investor definitions and the rise of new investment structures have opened doors. What was once reserved exclusively for institutions is now accessible to accredited individuals who meet specific income or net-worth requirements.

The bottom line: Alternative investments are no longer just for institutions. Accredited investors can now build diversified portfolios that include exposure to private companies, private credit, real estate, and other alternative assets—all alongside their traditional public market holdings.

Registered Funds vs. Private Funds

Key differences between registered and private fund structures

What Are Registered Funds and How They Differ from Private Funds

Alternative investments have traditionally been the domain of institutional investors and ultra-high-net-worth individuals accessing private funds with high minimums, long lockups, and limited oversight. Registered funds are changing that equation, bringing alternative investment strategies to a broader investor base through structures designed for accessibility, transparency, and regulatory protection.

Understanding the difference between registered and private funds helps you determine which structure best serves your clients' needs and circumstances.

The Fundamental Difference

The core distinction comes down to regulatory framework and investor protections.

Private funds operate under exemptions from securities registration requirements—typically Regulation D or Section 3(c)(1) and 3(c)(7) of the Investment Company Act of 1940. These exemptions allow fund managers flexibility in strategy, fees, and operations, but limit who can invest (typically accredited investors or qualified purchasers) and impose minimal disclosure requirements.

Registered funds are securities registered with the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933 and regulated under the Investment Company Act of 1940. This registration subjects them to comprehensive disclosure requirements, operational standards, and investor protections.

The trade-off is straightforward: registered funds sacrifice some operational flexibility in exchange for broader accessibility and standardized investor protections.

Common Registered Fund Structures for Alternatives

Several registered fund structures provide access to alternative strategies:

Interval Funds

Interval funds are closed-end funds that offer periodic redemption windows (typically quarterly) for a limited percentage of fund assets (usually 5-25%). Between these windows, shares are illiquid.

This structure works well for alternative strategies requiring some illiquidity—private credit, real estate, infrastructure—where the underlying investments can't be sold immediately but managers can plan for periodic liquidity needs.

You invest $100,000 in an interval fund offering quarterly redemptions of up to 10% of fund assets. Each quarter, you can request redemption of your shares, though the fund may prorate requests if total redemptions exceed the 10% limit.

Interval funds typically have lower minimums ($2,500-$25,000) than private funds and provide more frequent valuation and reporting.

Tender Offer Funds

Similar to interval funds, tender offer funds periodically offer to repurchase shares from investors. The key difference: the fund controls when and how much to repurchase, rather than offering regular quarterly windows.

Tender offers might occur quarterly, semi-annually, or opportunistically when the manager determines liquidity is available. This gives managers more control over capital flows but less predictability for investors.

Business Development Companies (BDCs)

BDCs are specialized registered investment companies that invest in small and mid-sized private companies, primarily through debt investments. Most BDCs trade on stock exchanges, providing daily liquidity.

BDCs must distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders as dividends, making them attractive for income-focused investors. They provide transparency through public filings and quarterly earnings calls, though trading prices can disconnect from net asset value.

Registered Closed-End Funds

Traditional closed-end funds issue a fixed number of shares in an initial offering, then trade on exchanges. Some invest in alternative strategies like infrastructure, real estate, or private credit.

These funds offer daily liquidity but trade at market prices that may diverge from NAV, sometimes at significant premiums or discounts.

'40 Act Funds with Alternative Strategies

Some mutual funds and ETFs registered under the 1940 Act employ alternative strategies—long-short equity, managed futures, event-driven—while maintaining daily liquidity. These "liquid alts" provide alternatives exposure without lockups, though return profiles may differ from pure private funds due to liquidity constraints.

Key Differences in Practice

Understanding how registered and private funds differ operationally helps you set appropriate client expectations:

Investor Eligibility

Private funds typically restrict investment to:

Accredited investors (income >$200K or net worth >$1M excluding primary residence)

Qualified purchasers (investments >$5M) for 3(c)(7) funds

Registered funds are available to all investors meeting minimum investment requirements, typically $2,500-$25,000. This democratization makes alternatives accessible to a broader client base.

Minimum Investments

Private funds often require $100K-$1M minimums, limiting access to wealthy clients. Registered funds typically start at $2,500-$25,000, allowing broader portfolio diversification across alternative strategies.

Liquidity Terms

Private funds impose multi-year lockups (often 5-10 years) with limited early withdrawal rights, usually subject to penalties.

Registered interval and tender offer funds provide periodic liquidity (quarterly or semi-annually), though redemptions may be limited to 5-25% of assets per period. BDCs and closed-end funds offer daily liquidity through exchange trading, though at market prices rather than NAV.

Fee Structures

Both structures charge management fees and performance fees, but presentation differs:

Private funds typically charge 1-2% management fees plus 15-20% performance fees (carried interest) above hurdle rates.

Registered funds disclose fees as expense ratios in prospectuses. Interval funds commonly charge 2-3% management fees and 10-20% performance fees, presented as total expense ratios and performance allocations. BDCs charge management fees plus incentive fees on income and gains.

The total economic cost may be similar, but registered fund fee disclosure is more standardized and transparent.

Regulatory Oversight and Reporting

This is where differences become most significant:

Private Funds:

Limited disclosure requirements, often just annual audited financials

Quarterly statements to investors

No required prospectus or standardized offering documents

Fewer restrictions on investment activities and conflicts of interest

Registered Funds:

Detailed prospectus required, updated annually

Statement of Additional Information (SAI) providing expanded disclosure

Quarterly and annual reports to shareholders

Daily NAV calculation and publication (for daily liquidity funds)

Restrictions on affiliate transactions and conflicts

Board of directors/trustees including independent members overseeing manager

SEC examination and oversight

Clients receive substantially more information and protection with registered funds, though this comes with operational costs reflected in expense ratios.

Valuation and Transparency

Private funds typically value portfolios quarterly, with significant discretion in valuing illiquid holdings. NAV is estimated rather than marked to market.

Registered interval funds value portfolios at least quarterly, following specific valuation procedures approved by the board. BDCs and exchange-traded closed-end funds value portfolios at least quarterly with significant disclosure around methodology.

While both structures face valuation challenges for illiquid assets, registered funds must follow standardized procedures with board oversight.

Tax Reporting

Private funds issue K-1 forms reporting partnership income, gains, and deductions. K-1s arrive late (often March or April), include complex allocations, and may trigger multi-state filing requirements.

Registered funds structured as corporations (like BDCs) issue simpler 1099-DIV forms for dividend income. Registered funds structured as partnerships still issue K-1s, but some use blockers to simplify taxation for certain investors.

The tax treatment depends more on fund structure (partnership vs. corporation) than registered vs. private status, but registered funds often prioritize simplified tax reporting.

When Registered Funds Make Sense

Registered alternative funds work well for clients who:

Need Some Liquidity

Clients uncomfortable locking up capital for 10 years benefit from interval funds' quarterly redemption windows or BDCs' daily liquidity, even if these options are imperfect.

Have Smaller Capital Allocations

The $2,500-$25,000 minimums allow diversification across multiple alternative strategies rather than concentrating in one $100K+ private fund commitment.

Value Regulatory Protections

Risk-averse clients appreciate standardized disclosure, board oversight, and SEC regulation, accepting somewhat higher expense ratios for these protections.

Prefer Simpler Tax Reporting

Those frustrated by K-1 complexity may prefer registered funds structured as corporations issuing 1099s.

Want Alternatives Exposure Without Illiquidity

Clients dipping toes into alternatives may start with interval funds or BDCs offering some liquidity before committing to fully illiquid private funds.

When Private Funds Remain Preferable

Private funds continue serving clients who:

Can Commit Capital Long-Term

Illiquidity allows managers to invest in assets with superior return potential without worrying about redemptions. Clients with 10+ year horizons may achieve better returns accepting full illiquidity.

Meet Higher Minimums Comfortably

Concentrated positions in carefully selected private funds with institutional-quality managers may outperform diversified registered fund approaches.

Access Top-Tier Managers

The best private equity, real estate, and credit managers often don't offer registered products, preferring traditional partnership structures.

Seek Maximum Tax Efficiency

Partnership structures offer tax planning opportunities (depreciation, tax credits) that corporate registered funds can't match.

Value Operational Flexibility

Private funds can pursue opportunistic strategies, concentrated positions, and flexible capital deployment that registered fund restrictions prohibit.

The Convergence Trend

The line between registered and private funds is blurring. High-quality alternative managers increasingly offer both:

Private funds for institutional investors and wealthy individuals seeking maximum returns with full illiquidity

Registered interval funds or BDCs bringing similar strategies to broader audiences with modified liquidity terms

This trend democratizes alternatives access while allowing clients to choose structures matching their liquidity needs and regulatory preferences.

Many managers find their registered and private strategies perform similarly over time, suggesting the structures can coexist effectively.

Making the Choice

The decision between registered and private alternative funds isn't binary. Consider:

Portfolio Allocation: Use registered funds for initial alternatives exposure or clients with smaller allocations. Graduate to private funds as allocations grow and conviction increases.

Strategy Fit: Some strategies (private credit, real estate debt) work well in interval funds. Others (venture capital, opportunistic real estate) require full illiquidity of private structures.

Client Circumstances: Liquidity needs, tax situations, minimum investment capacity, and regulatory preferences drive individual decisions.

Manager Quality: Choose the best manager for the strategy regardless of structure. A top-tier registered fund may outperform a mediocre private fund.

The Bottom Line

Registered alternative funds make institutional-quality strategies accessible to a broader investor base through standardized structures, regulatory protections, and improved liquidity. They sacrifice some return potential and operational flexibility compared to private funds, but offer meaningful benefits for many clients.

Understanding the structural differences helps you match fund types to client circumstances. For some clients, registered interval funds or BDCs provide ideal alternatives exposure. For others, traditional private funds better serve long-term wealth building.

The expansion of registered alternatives represents progress, expanding access to strategies once reserved for institutions and the ultra-wealthy. Both structures have their place in comprehensive alternatives allocations.

What Do SPVs Do?

Understanding Special Purpose Vehicles and their role

When you invest in a pre-IPO company through a platform, your investment goes through a Special Purpose Vehicle—an SPV. Understanding what SPVs are and how they work is essential. These vehicles make pre-IPO investing possible for individual investors, bridging the gap between you and private company shares.

SPVs Solve a Real Problem

Here's the challenge: Private companies don't want hundreds of individual investors on their cap table. It creates administrative burdens, complicates governance, and makes future fundraising more difficult.

At the same time, individual investors can't meet the minimum investment sizes private companies require. A single share purchase might require $20 million, $100 million, or more. That's not accessible to most investors.

SPVs solve both problems. They're legal entities—typically Delaware limited liability companies (LLCs)—created for one specific purpose: to pool capital from multiple investors and purchase shares of a private company.

By aggregating capital, SPVs can meet the minimums needed to transact. And instead of adding dozens or hundreds of new names to their cap table, private companies see just one entity: the SPV.

What You Actually Own

When you invest in an SPV, you're not buying shares of the private company directly. You're buying membership interests in the SPV, which owns shares of the company.

Here's how it works:

An SPV raises $1 million from investors and uses that capital to purchase 10,000 shares of Company X at $100 per share. If you invested $10,000, you own 1% of the SPV. That 1% translates to indirect ownership of 100 shares of Company X (1% of the SPV's 10,000 shares).

Your ownership is formalized through legal documents you sign during the investment process—particularly the Subscription Agreement. These documents spell out:

Your proportional ownership of the underlying shares

Your rights to distributions when the company exits

Any voting or governance rights that flow through the SPV

Your obligations as an SPV member

Generally, SPVs will not provide voting, governance rights, or other obligations to end investors. This is to minimize investor administrative overhead, and provide the cleaning "like-public markets" investment experience. With nearly 50% of Pre-IPO volume transacted in SPVs through 2025, they have been solidified as the de facto method of transacting in Pre-IPO.

How SPVs Protect Your Investment

Several mechanisms ensure your ownership is protected:

Company Approval

Every private company approves the transfer of shares to the SPV. This isn't just a formality—it's the company formally acknowledging the SPV's ownership. That acknowledgment affirms your indirect ownership as an SPV member.

Most private companies have a Right of First Refusal (ROFR), which means they have the option to buy shares before they're sold to a third party. Reputable platforms have spent years building relationships with private companies, earning their trust and streamlining this approval process.

Legal Documentation

The Subscription Agreement and related documents create binding legal rights to the SPV's assets. Your proportional ownership and distribution rights are clearly established. These documents are accessible through your investment account—they're not hidden or obscured.

Regulatory Oversight

Platforms facilitating SPV investments operate as registered broker-dealers and Investment Advisors under SEC and FINRA oversight. This regulatory framework provides investor protections and ensures transactions follow securities laws.

Transaction Diligence

Reputable platforms perform diligence on each transaction to make sure SPVs actually purchase and hold the shares as represented. This includes verifying transfers, maintaining custody, and ensuring all legal requirements are met.

How SPVs Work from Start to Finish

Understanding the SPV lifecycle helps you know what to expect:

1. Formation

The SPV is established as a legal entity to purchase shares of one specific private company. Each SPV is separate—if you invest in SPVs for both Company X and Company Y, those are two distinct legal entities.

2. Fundraising

Accredited investors commit capital by purchasing membership interests in the SPV. This typically happens over a few weeks as the platform syndicates the deal.

3. Share Purchase

Once enough capital is raised, the SPV purchases shares from existing shareholders—often employees, early investors, or other parties looking for liquidity.

4. Company Approval

The private company reviews and approves the share transfer through its Right of First Refusal process. This is when the company formally acknowledges the SPV's ownership.

5. Holding Period

The SPV holds the shares on behalf of investors. This typically 1 year prior to secondary trading with a full SPV lifecycle that lasts 2-10 years until a liquidity event—an IPO, acquisition, or other exit.

6. Exit

When the company goes public, gets acquired, or another liquidity event occurs, the SPV distributes proceeds to investors based on proportional ownership.

Throughout this entire process, you maintain your proportional ownership rights. If the company undergoes corporate actions—stock splits, mergers, additional funding rounds—the SPV's holdings (and therefore your indirect ownership) adjust accordingly.

Why SPVs Matter

The SPV structure has fundamentally changed who can access pre-IPO investing. What was once limited to venture capital firms and ultra-high-net-worth individuals is now available to accredited investors with minimums as low as $5,000-$10,000.

By efficiently pooling capital and simplifying private company cap tables, SPVs created a scalable model for bringing institutional-quality opportunities to individual investors.

For you, this means access to carefully vetted opportunities in innovative private companies with clear ownership structures and professional management. For private companies, it means access to a broader investor base without the headache of managing hundreds of individual shareholders.

The SPV structure makes pre-IPO investing work—for everyone.

What is Pre-IPO Investing?

How pre-IPO markets work and why they matter

Pre-IPO investing means buying shares in private companies before they go public. But we're not talking about early-stage startups here. Pre-IPO companies are typically high-growth, venture-backed businesses that have already proven their business model, built substantial revenue, and established themselves in their markets. Think companies like SpaceX, OpenAI, or Anthropic—they're private, but they're not small.

These investments offer accredited investors a chance to access some of the world's most innovative companies during a critical growth phase, before those companies become available on public stock exchanges.

Why Pre-IPO Returns Can Be Different

Pre-IPO investments come with the potential for higher returns than public stocks. That premium comes from three main factors:

The Illiquidity Premium

When you buy a public stock, you can sell it the next day. When you invest in a pre-IPO company, your money is typically locked up for 2-10 years until the company goes public or gets acquired.

Investors expect—and historically have received—higher returns as compensation for this lack of liquidity. This "illiquidity premium" is real and it's priced into private company valuations. You're giving up flexibility in exchange for the potential for outsized returns.

Limited Transparency

Private companies aren't required to disclose nearly as much information as public companies. No quarterly earnings calls. No 10-Ks or 10-Qs. This creates risk, but for investors who do their homework, it also creates opportunity.

The information gap means you need to dig deeper and understand what you're buying. But it also means you can identify great companies before the rest of the market catches on.

Access to Exceptional Growth

Here's the reality: For certain innovative companies, pre-IPO investing is the only way individual investors can participate in their growth.

By the time many companies go public, their explosive growth phase is already behind them. Companies that IPO today have often been private for over a decade, building value that early investors captured but public market investors missed entirely.

If you want exposure to the next generation of transformative companies—the ones building AI, reinventing industries, or creating new markets—you often need to invest before they're public.

Why This Matters Now

The investment landscape has fundamentally changed. Over the last 30 years:

The number of public companies has dropped by 50%

The number of private companies has grown by 43%

The average time a company stays private before IPO has gone from 5 years to 13 years

What does this mean for you? Some of today's most important companies are building massive value as private companies. Over 19,000 private U.S. companies generate more than $100 million in revenue. Twenty years ago, most of them would have already gone public. Today, they're staying private longer, accessing private capital markets without the regulatory burden of being public.

If you want exposure to the companies shaping the future, you need access to private markets.

How Pre-IPO Investing Actually Works

Modern pre-IPO investing happens through Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs)—also called single-company funds. Here's how it works:

Pooling Capital

Individual investors contribute capital to an SPV. The SPV aggregates this money to purchase shares of a specific private company. This structure solves a practical problem: private companies don't want dozens or hundreds of individual shareholders on their cap table. An SPV shows up as one entity, making everyone's life easier.

The Transaction Process

Most pre-IPO transactions are secondary transactions—meaning you're buying existing shares from current shareholders (often employees or early investors) rather than new shares directly from the company.

The process includes:

Sourcing shares from selling shareholders

Getting company approval (most private companies have a Right of First Refusal)

Managing regulatory compliance

Handling the actual transfer of shares

Platforms like Monark handle all of this, but it still takes time—typically 1-4 weeks from investment to close.

Your Ownership

When you invest in an SPV, you're buying membership interests in that SPV. The SPV owns shares of the private company. You own your pro-rata portion of whatever the SPV holds.

If the SPV owns 10,000 shares and you invested $10,000 out of a $1 million SPV, you own 1%—which translates to indirect ownership of 100 shares of the underlying company.

What Investment Opportunities Look Like

Accredited investors can access pre-IPO companies through several structures:

Single-Company Funds

This is the most common option. You invest in one specific private company through an SPV. Investment minimums typically start at $10,000, though some platforms offer $5,000 minimums for select deals.

You're making a concentrated bet on one company, which means you should understand that business, its market, and its growth prospects.

Multi-Company Funds

Want diversification? Multi-company funds spread your capital across multiple private companies, reducing the risk that any single company drags down your returns.

These funds help you build exposure to the pre-IPO asset class without concentrating all your risk in one name.

Direct Investments

For larger investors, direct purchases from selling shareholders are sometimes available, though these typically require significantly higher minimums.

How to Get Started

Investing in pre-IPO companies involves three steps:

Step 1: Verify You're Accredited

Pre-IPO investments are limited to accredited investors. You qualify if you have:

Annual income of at least $200,000 (or $300,000 combined with a spouse) for the last two years, with expectation of the same for the current year

Net worth exceeding $1 million, excluding your primary residence

Certain professional certifications (Series 7, 65, or 82)

Step 2: Discover Companies and Indicate Interest

Browse available opportunities on platforms that offer pre-IPO access. New single-company funds typically launch on regular schedules (for example, Monark launches new deals every Tuesday and Thursday).

When you find companies you're interested in, you can indicate your interest or add it to your watchlist. This signals demand to the platform and ensures you get notified when new opportunities become available.

Step 3: Complete Your Investment

Once you find an opportunity you want to invest in:

Review the offering documents (company information, cap table, risks)

Complete a non-binding term sheet to reserve your allocation

Sign the subscription agreement (this is binding)

Provide funding via ACH or wire transfer

Wait for company approval and the closing process

The whole process takes 6-10 weeks from investment to close, but you'll have visibility into where things stand throughout.

What to Keep in Mind

Pre-IPO investing offers compelling opportunities, but it's not without risks:

Your money is illiquid for years

Not all companies go public or get acquired

You could lose your entire investment

Information is more limited than with public stocks

That said, for accredited investors looking to access high-growth companies and potentially achieve returns that outperform public markets, pre-IPO investing has become an increasingly important part of a diversified portfolio.

The key is understanding what you're buying, doing your research, and only investing capital you can afford to have locked up for several years.

What Private Equity Is and Why It Matters

Private equity involves investing in companies that aren't publicly traded. PE firms raise capital from investors, use that capital to acquire companies, work to improve those businesses over 3-7 years, then sell them for a profit.

Unlike public stocks where you buy shares on an exchange, PE investments happen through funds. You commit capital to a fund managed by a General Partner (GP), who identifies companies to buy, manages those investments, and eventually exits them. Your returns depend on how well the GP executes this strategy.

PE has historically outperformed public markets over the long term, though with significantly less liquidity. The asset class works because PE firms can take a longer-term view than public company executives focused on quarterly earnings, implement operational improvements without public market scrutiny, and use leverage strategically to enhance returns.

For advisors, PE represents a way to give clients access to a broader universe of companies and potentially higher returns in exchange for illiquidity and higher minimums.

Investment Structures and Vehicles

Traditional Closed-End Funds

Most PE investments happen through closed-end limited partnerships. Here's how they work:

The GP raises a target amount (say, $500 million) over 12-18 months. Once the fundraising period closes, no new investors can enter. The fund has a fixed life, typically 10-12 years with possible extensions.

You don't invest your full commitment upfront. Instead, you commit capital and the GP "calls" it over time as investment opportunities arise. If you commit $100,000, you might contribute $25,000 in year one, $30,000 in year two, and so on. These capital calls typically occur with 10-14 days' notice.

The GP uses committed capital to acquire companies, improve operations, and eventually sell those businesses. As exits occur, the fund distributes proceeds to investors. Your returns come from these distributions, not from selling your fund position.

Evergreen Funds

Some PE firms now offer evergreen structures without fixed end dates. These funds continuously raise capital, make investments, and provide periodic liquidity windows for investors who want to exit. They solve the traditional PE challenge of having capital locked up for a decade, but may trade some return potential for that flexibility.

Interval Funds

Registered as 1940 Act funds, interval funds offer PE exposure with quarterly or semi-annual redemption windows. These provide more liquidity than traditional PE funds but limit redemptions to a percentage of fund assets (typically 5-25% quarterly).

Fund of Funds

These vehicles invest in multiple PE funds rather than directly in companies. They provide instant diversification across managers, strategies, and vintage years, but add an additional layer of fees.

Investment Strategies and Focus Areas

PE firms typically specialize in specific strategies:

Buyouts: Acquiring controlling stakes in established companies, often using leverage. Buyout funds focus on operational improvements, strategic repositioning, and multiple expansion. This is the largest segment of PE by capital deployed.

Growth Equity: Minority investments in mature companies seeking capital for expansion. Less leverage than buyouts, focusing on companies with proven business models ready to scale.

Venture Capital: Early-stage investments in high-growth potential startups. Higher risk and failure rates, but potentially outsized returns from successful companies.

Distressed/Special Situations: Investing in companies facing financial or operational challenges. Requires specialized expertise in turnarounds and restructuring.

Sector-Focused: Some funds concentrate on specific industries (healthcare, technology, industrials) where they've built deep expertise and networks.

Understanding a fund's strategy helps you assess whether it aligns with your clients' risk tolerance and return objectives.

Due Diligence: What to Evaluate

Track Record and Performance

Review the GP's historical returns across multiple fund vintages. Look at:

Net IRR (Internal Rate of Return) after all fees

TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In) showing total value created relative to capital invested

DPI (Distributions to Paid-In) showing actual cash returned vs. unrealized gains

Performance relative to benchmarks and peers

Consistency across economic cycles

Be cautious of funds with limited track records or those showing only unrealized gains without distributions.

Team Experience and Stability

The investment team drives performance. Evaluate:

Years of experience in PE and relevant industries

Team stability and turnover rates

Deal sourcing capabilities and networks

Operational expertise for value creation

Alignment through GP commitment (how much the team invests personally)

Strong teams have continuity, complementary skills, and skin in the game.

Investment Process

Understand how the GP sources, evaluates, and manages investments:

Deal flow sources and competitive advantages

Due diligence rigor and specialist resources

Value creation playbooks and operational support

Portfolio monitoring and reporting

Exit strategy development

Disciplined, repeatable processes produce more consistent results than one-off successes.

Fee Structure

PE funds typically charge:

Management fees: 1.5-2% annually on committed or invested capital

Carried interest (carry): 20% of profits above a hurdle rate (often 8%)

Transaction and monitoring fees from portfolio companies

These fees compound over time. A fund with strong gross returns but excessive fees may underperform on a net basis.

Fund Terms

Review key terms in the Limited Partnership Agreement:

Fund size and minimum investment

Investment period (typically 4-6 years)

Fund life (typically 10-12 years)

GP commitment requirement

Key person provisions

Clawback provisions protecting LPs

Fee Structures and Economics

Understanding PE economics helps you evaluate whether returns justify the costs.

The 2/20 Model

Traditional PE charges 2% management fees annually and 20% carried interest on profits. Here's what that means:

On a $100,000 commitment, you might pay $2,000 annually in management fees during the investment period. If the fund generates $200,000 in gains, the GP takes 20% ($40,000) as carry, leaving you with $160,000 in gains.

Many funds now have tiered fee structures—lower management fees after the investment period ends, or fees that decline as capital is returned.

Hurdle Rates

Most funds include a preferred return or hurdle rate—LPs receive all returns up to this threshold (often 8%) before the GP receives any carry. This aligns interests by ensuring GPs only participate in gains after delivering minimum returns.

Fee Offsets

GPs often charge portfolio companies transaction fees, monitoring fees, or directors' fees. Quality funds offset 50-100% of these fees against management fees owed by LPs. Without offsets, you're effectively paying double fees.

Risk Factors and Considerations

Illiquidity

PE investments typically lock up capital for 7-12 years. You cannot access your capital during this period except through secondary markets (often at discounts). Only invest capital your clients won't need for a decade or more.

Capital Call Risk

When the GP calls capital, you must fund within 10-14 days. Failure to fund can result in penalties or forfeiture of interests. Ensure clients maintain liquidity reserves to meet unfunded commitments.

Market Risk

PE values fluctuate with market conditions. Entry timing matters—funds investing at market peaks may generate lower returns. Vintage year diversification helps mitigate this risk.

Operational Risk

PE returns depend on the GP's ability to improve portfolio companies. Poor operational execution, failed add-on acquisitions, or strategic missteps can destroy value.

Leverage Risk

Most buyout funds use 50-70% debt in acquisitions. While leverage amplifies returns in good times, it increases downside risk during recessions or rising interest rate environments.

Concentration Risk

A single failed investment can significantly impact fund returns, especially in smaller funds with 10-15 companies. Larger funds with 30+ investments provide more diversification.

Tax Implications

PE funds structured as partnerships have specific tax considerations:

K-1 Forms

You'll receive Schedule K-1s annually, reporting your share of fund income, gains, and deductions. K-1s typically arrive in March or April, potentially delaying tax filing.

Pass-Through Taxation

Income and gains flow through to your tax return annually, even if you haven't received distributions. You may owe taxes before receiving cash.

Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI)

If the fund uses leverage, tax-exempt entities (IRAs, foundations) may face UBTI, creating unexpected tax obligations.

Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS)

Some PE investments may qualify for QSBS treatment, potentially excluding up to 100% of gains from federal taxes. This is more common in growth equity and venture capital.

Long-Term Capital Gains

Most PE gains receive favorable long-term capital gains treatment since holding periods typically exceed one year.

Work with tax advisors familiar with partnership taxation to properly plan for these implications.

Client Suitability Assessment

Not every client is appropriate for PE investments. Consider:

Accredited or Qualified Status

Most PE funds require investors to be accredited investors (income >$200K or net worth >$1M excluding primary residence) or qualified purchasers (investments >$5M).

Liquidity Needs

Clients must have sufficient liquid assets to meet unfunded commitments and maintain emergency reserves. A common guideline: PE should represent no more than 10-20% of liquid net worth.

Time Horizon

Clients need 10+ year time horizons. Those approaching retirement or needing capital soon are poor candidates.

Risk Tolerance

PE involves company-specific risk, leverage risk, and illiquidity. Clients must be comfortable with volatility and potential loss of capital.

Sophistication

Clients should understand the commitment structure, J-curve effect (early negative returns as fees are paid before gains materialize), and the importance of vintage year diversification.

Portfolio Construction Considerations

Allocation Sizing

Institutional investors typically allocate 10-30% to PE. Individual investors might target 5-15% depending on liquidity needs and risk tolerance. Start smaller and build over time.

Diversification Strategy

Spread commitments across:

Multiple vintage years (commit to 2-3 funds annually rather than concentrating in one year)

Different strategies (buyout, growth, venture)

Various sectors and geographies

Different manager sizes and styles

This diversification smooths the J-curve and reduces manager-specific risk.

Pacing Strategy

Rather than committing all at once, develop a multi-year pacing plan. Commit to new funds consistently over 3-5 years to diversify vintage years and avoid market timing risk.

Rebalancing Challenges

PE positions can't be easily rebalanced like public stocks. As distributions occur, develop plans for reinvesting proceeds to maintain target allocations.

Performance Benchmarking

Relevant Benchmarks

Common PE benchmarks include:

Cambridge Associates Private Equity Index

Burgiss Private iQ Indices

State Street Private Equity Index

Public market equivalents (PME) comparing PE to S&P 500 returns

Compare funds to appropriate benchmarks based on strategy and vintage year.

Key Metrics

IRR: Time-weighted return accounting for timing of cash flows

TVPI: Total value (distributed + NAV) divided by paid-in capital

DPI: Distributions divided by paid-in capital (measures actual cash returned)

RVPI: Residual value (unrealized NAV) divided by paid-in capital

Strong funds show top-quartile performance across multiple vintages, consistent DPI generation, and TVPI exceeding 2.0x.

Key Takeaways for Advisors

Private equity offers clients access to a broader investment universe and potentially superior long-term returns. Success requires careful GP selection, proper portfolio construction, and clients who understand the commitment structure and illiquidity.

Start with proven, established managers with strong track records. Build diversified PE portfolios over multiple years. Ensure clients have the financial capacity and temperament for long-term illiquid investments. When implemented thoughtfully, PE can meaningfully enhance portfolio returns and provide access to attractive companies unavailable in public markets.

Private Credit

What Private Credit Is and Why It Matters

Private credit involves lending directly to companies outside traditional bank channels. Instead of companies borrowing from banks or issuing bonds in public markets, they borrow from specialized credit funds that provide customized financing solutions.

The private credit market has grown dramatically since the 2008 financial crisis as banks retreated from certain lending activities due to regulatory changes. This created opportunity for non-bank lenders to fill the gap, providing capital to middle-market companies (typically $50M-$500M in revenue) that need flexible financing.

For investors, private credit offers attractive yields (typically 8-12%) with lower volatility than equities, floating rate structures that protect against rising interest rates, and seniority in the capital structure providing downside protection. Unlike bonds that trade daily, private credit loans are held to maturity, reducing mark-to-market volatility.

For advisors, private credit provides clients with higher income than traditional fixed income, floating rate exposure during rising rate environments, and diversification from public credit markets.

Investment Structures and Vehicles

Closed-End Credit Funds

Traditional private credit funds operate as closed-end limited partnerships. Investors commit capital that's called as loan opportunities arise over a 3-5 year investment period. The fund deploys capital into loans, collects interest income and principal repayments, then distributes proceeds to investors over the fund's 7-10 year life.

These funds typically begin distributing income within 6-12 months as loans are originated and start generating interest payments.

Interval Funds

Registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940, interval funds offer private credit exposure with limited liquidity. They provide quarterly or semi-annual redemption windows (typically 5-25% of fund assets) while holding portfolios of private loans.

Interval funds appeal to investors seeking higher yields than public credit with more liquidity than traditional private funds.

Business Development Companies (BDCs)

BDCs are publicly traded closed-end funds that invest in private credit. They trade on exchanges with daily liquidity, pay out 90% of income as dividends (required for tax benefits), and provide transparency through public filings.

BDCs offer easy access to private credit but trade at market prices that may disconnect from net asset value during volatility.

Evergreen Funds

Some managers offer perpetual structures without fixed end dates. These funds continuously raise capital, originate loans, and provide periodic redemption opportunities, combining aspects of closed-end and open-end structures.

Separately Managed Accounts (SMAs)

Larger investors ($10M+ minimums) may establish SMAs with credit managers, providing full transparency into underlying loans, customized portfolio construction, and direct ownership of loan assets.

Credit Strategies and Lending Types

Private credit encompasses diverse lending strategies:

Direct Lending (Sponsored)

Loans to private equity-backed companies, typically for leveraged buyouts, acquisitions, or refinancings. Direct lending is the largest private credit segment, characterized by:

Secured senior debt with first priority claims on assets

Floating rates (typically SOFR + 5-8%)

Loan-to-value ratios of 40-60%

5-7 year maturities

Financial maintenance covenants protecting lenders

Direct lending to PE-backed companies benefits from sponsor support and operational improvements.

Direct Lending (Non-Sponsored)

Loans to founder-owned, family-owned, or independent companies without PE backing. These situations often involve:

Business succession financing

Growth capital for expansion

Acquisition financing for strategic buyers

Recapitalizations

Non-sponsored lending requires deeper underwriting given the absence of professional PE oversight but can offer higher yields.

Unitranche

A hybrid structure combining senior and subordinated debt in a single loan, simplifying capital structures. Unitranche loans typically price at SOFR + 6-9%, offering higher yields than senior loans with moderate additional risk.

Mezzanine Debt

Subordinated, unsecured loans ranking below senior debt but above equity. Mezzanine typically includes equity participation (warrants or equity co-investment) alongside 10-14% cash interest, targeting total returns of 12-18%.

Mezzanine suits companies needing growth capital without diluting equity ownership significantly.

Asset-Based Lending (ABL)

Loans secured by specific assets—inventory, accounts receivable, equipment, or real estate. ABL provides working capital flexibility for companies with valuable tangible assets, often at lower rates than cash flow-based lending due to hard asset collateral.

Specialty Finance

Niche lending strategies targeting specific sectors or situations:

Real estate debt: Construction loans, bridge financing, mezzanine for property developments

Venture debt: Loans to venture-backed technology companies supplementing equity funding

Infrastructure debt: Financing for infrastructure projects with stable cash flows

Aviation/equipment finance: Loans secured by aircraft, machinery, or equipment

Consumer finance: Portfolios of consumer loans (auto, personal, student)

Distressed/Opportunistic Credit

Loans to or purchases of debt from financially stressed companies. Distressed credit targets 15-20%+ returns through:

Acquiring debt at discounts in bankruptcy or restructuring

Providing rescue financing (debtor-in-possession loans)

Loan-to-own strategies acquiring equity through foreclosure

Distressed credit requires specialized workout expertise and higher risk tolerance.

Due Diligence: What to Evaluate

Manager Experience and Platform

Private credit requires specialized origination and underwriting capabilities:

Origination Channels: Proprietary deal flow from PE relationships, intermediaries, or direct company relationships generates better terms than competitive auctions

Underwriting Discipline: Historical default rates, loss severity, and net loss experience demonstrate credit quality

Portfolio Management: Active monitoring, early problem identification, and workout capabilities

Team Depth: Experienced credit professionals with full economic cycles of experience

Operational Infrastructure: Legal, documentation, compliance, and servicing capabilities

Credit Fundamentals and Underwriting

Assess how managers evaluate borrower creditworthiness:

Cash Flow Analysis: Sustainable EBITDA, free cash flow generation, debt service coverage ratios

Industry Dynamics: Competitive positioning, barriers to entry, end-market exposure

Management Quality: Track record, industry expertise, succession planning

Collateral Coverage: Liquidation value of assets securing loans, advance rates

Covenant Structures: Financial maintenance covenants providing early warning signals

Strong managers pass on deals that don't meet strict credit criteria despite yield pressure.

Portfolio Construction and Diversification

Review how funds build portfolios:

Diversification: 50-100+ loans across industries, borrowers, and geographies

Concentration Limits: Maximum exposure per borrower (typically 3-5%)

Industry Exposures: Avoiding cyclical industry concentration

Loan Size: Lending to established companies versus smaller, riskier businesses

Covenant Coverage: Percentage of portfolio with maintenance covenants

Historical Performance

Evaluate track record across cycles:

Gross vs. Net Returns: Returns before and after fees and losses

Default Rates: Historical defaults relative to portfolio size

Loss Given Default: Recovery rates on defaulted loans

Income Consistency: Stability of quarterly distributions through market cycles

Quality managers show consistent income generation, low default rates (typically <2% annually), and strong recovery rates (60-80% of par).

Fee Structures and Economics

Private credit fees vary by structure and strategy:

Management Fees

Most credit funds charge 1.0-1.5% annually on committed capital during the investment period, then on invested capital or NAV during the harvest period. This is lower than PE but higher than public credit given the active origination and monitoring required.

Performance Fees

Credit funds typically charge smaller performance fees than PE:

Hurdle rates of 6-8% (lower than PE's 8% given lower risk)

Carried interest of 10-15% (lower than PE's typical 20%)

Some funds use catch-up provisions allowing GPs to catch up to their full carry percentage after LPs receive the hurdle

Fee Structures by Vehicle Type

Closed-End Funds: Traditional management fees plus carry

Interval Funds: Management fees of 1.5-2.5% with performance fees of 10-15%

BDCs: Management fees of 1.5-2.0% plus incentive fees (typically 20% of income and gains above hurdle)

Alignment Features

Look for GP commitments of 2-5% of fund capital and clawback provisions ensuring managers don't over-earn carry if early profits are followed by later losses.

Risk Factors and Considerations

Credit Risk

Borrowers may default due to business deterioration, industry headwinds, or macroeconomic stress. While private credit typically experiences lower default rates than high-yield bonds (1-3% annually vs. 3-5%), losses still occur.

Mitigate through:

Diversification across 50+ borrowers

Conservative underwriting focused on cash flow coverage

Strong collateral coverage and covenant protections

Active portfolio monitoring and early problem identification

Interest Rate Risk

Unlike fixed-rate bonds that decline in value when rates rise, most private credit uses floating rates (SOFR + spread). This protects against rising rates but exposes investors to lower yields if rates decline.

However, most loans have SOFR floors (typically 0-1%) preventing yields from falling below minimum levels.

Illiquidity Risk

Private credit investments typically lock up capital for 7-10 years in closed-end funds. While loans may mature in 5-7 years, the fund needs time to harvest all positions. Secondary markets exist but offer limited liquidity at uncertain pricing.

Only invest capital clients can commit long-term.

Market Cycle Risk

Private credit funds deployed during competitive lending markets (tight spreads, weak covenants, high leverage) may underperform if economic downturns occur. Vintage year matters significantly.

Favor managers with discipline to reduce deployment during frothy markets, accepting lower near-term yields to avoid problem loans.

Refinancing Risk

Borrowers may refinance loans early when credit markets improve, eliminating high-yielding loans from portfolios. Early payoffs reduce overall returns if proceeds cannot be reinvested at similar yields.

Operational and Documentation Risk

Private credit requires robust legal documentation, collateral perfection, and ongoing compliance. Poor documentation or monitoring can impair recovery in defaults.

Tax Implications

Private credit taxation depends on fund structure:

Partnership Structure (K-1)

Traditional credit funds issue K-1 forms reporting:

Ordinary income from interest payments (taxed at ordinary rates, not qualified dividends)

Capital gains/losses from loan sales or equity realizations

Possible state-source income requiring multi-state filings

Interest income receives ordinary income treatment, resulting in higher tax rates than qualified dividends or long-term capital gains.

BDC Structure (1099)

BDCs issue 1099-DIV forms reporting dividend income. While simpler than K-1s, BDC dividends are also taxed as ordinary income.

Tax-Deferred Accounts

Private credit works well in IRAs or other tax-deferred vehicles since ordinary income taxation can be deferred until withdrawal, and UBTI concerns are minimal for non-levered credit funds.

Non-US Investors

Non-US investors may face withholding taxes on interest income depending on treaty provisions and fund structures.

Client Suitability Assessment

Private credit suits clients seeking:

Enhanced Fixed Income Yields

With government bonds yielding 3-5%, private credit's 8-12% yields appeal to income-focused investors willing to accept illiquidity.

Floating Rate Exposure

Clients concerned about rising interest rates benefit from private credit's floating rate structures that increase income as rates rise.

Lower Volatility Than Equities

Private credit offers equity-like returns with bond-like volatility, appealing to moderate-risk investors.

Diversification from Public Markets

Private credit exhibits low correlation to public equities and bonds, providing diversification benefits.

Appropriate Liquidity Tolerance

Clients must accept 5-10 year lockups in closed-end funds or limited redemption windows in interval funds/BDCs.

Portfolio Construction Considerations

Strategic Allocation

Institutions typically allocate 5-15% to private credit. Individual investors might target 5-10% depending on income needs and fixed income allocation size.

Private credit can substitute for portions of traditional fixed income allocations, offering higher yields and floating rates while maintaining income focus.

Strategy Diversification

Blend strategies across the credit spectrum:

Senior direct lending: 50-60% (stable income, downside protection)

Unitranche/mezzanine: 20-30% (enhanced yields with moderate additional risk)

Specialty finance: 10-20% (diversification, niche opportunities)

Opportunistic/distressed: 5-10% (appreciation potential, higher risk)

Vintage Year Diversification

Commit to credit funds over multiple years to diversify entry points across credit cycles. Funds deployed during wide-spread markets (post-recession) often generate strong returns while tight-spread vintages may disappoint.

Manager Diversification

Spread commitments across 3-5 managers with different origination channels, industry focuses, and credit approaches to reduce manager-specific risk.

Liquid vs. Illiquid Balance

Combine closed-end credit funds with interval funds or BDCs to maintain some liquidity while capturing private credit premiums.

Relevant Benchmarks

Cliffwater Direct Lending Index: Broad private credit benchmark

ICE BofA High Yield Index: Public high-yield bonds

Custom benchmarks: SOFR + target spread reflecting strategy

Key Metrics

Current Yield: Annual interest income divided by invested capital

Total Return: Interest income plus principal gains/losses

Default Rate: Annual defaults as percentage of portfolio

Loss Rate: Net losses after recoveries as percentage of portfolio

Income Consistency: Quarterly distribution stability

Quality credit funds deliver:

Senior direct lending: 8-11% net returns, <2% annual defaults

Mezzanine: 12-15% net returns, 2-4% annual defaults

Consistent quarterly income with minimal principal impairment

Key Takeaways for Advisors

Private credit provides attractive, current income with floating rate exposure and lower volatility than equities. The asset class fills the gap between public bonds and private equity, offering bond-like predictability with enhanced yields.

Success requires selecting managers with disciplined underwriting, proprietary origination, and workout expertise. Understanding different credit strategies—senior lending for stability, mezzanine for yield enhancement, specialty finance for diversification—allows portfolio construction matching client objectives.

Private credit's combination of high current income, floating rate protection, and moderate risk makes it particularly valuable for income-focused clients in today's environment. For those with appropriate illiquidity tolerance, private credit deserves consideration as a core fixed income alternative.

What Private Real Estate Investing Is and Why It Matters

Private real estate investing involves direct or indirect ownership of physical properties—office buildings, apartments, warehouses, retail centers, hotels—that generate income through rents and appreciate through market dynamics and property improvements.

Unlike publicly traded REITs that trade daily on exchanges, private real estate funds own properties directly, hold them for 5-10 years, and provide limited liquidity. This illiquidity allows managers to focus on long-term value creation rather than responding to daily market sentiment.

Real estate offers three potential return sources: rental income (current yield), property appreciation (capital gains), and value creation through improvements and repositioning. Total returns historically average 8-12% annually depending on strategy and market cycle timing.

For advisors, private real estate provides portfolio diversification, inflation hedging, current income, and tangible asset backing that clients can understand intuitively.

Investment Structures and Vehicles

Closed-End Funds (Commingled Funds)

Most institutional real estate investing happens through closed-end funds. These funds raise capital over 12-24 months, deploy it into properties over 3-5 years, hold properties for value creation, then sell and distribute proceeds over the fund's 7-10 year life.

Investors commit capital upfront, though some funds call capital as properties are acquired. Unlike PE, real estate funds often begin distributing income from rental operations before the fund exits all properties.

Open-End Funds (Core Funds)

Open-end funds offer ongoing subscriptions and redemptions (typically quarterly) while maintaining portfolios of stabilized properties. These funds provide more liquidity but may impose redemption queues during market stress when property sales cannot keep pace with redemption requests.

Core funds typically target current income (4-6% yields) with modest appreciation, appealing to investors seeking real estate exposure with more flexibility.

REITs (Public vs. Private)

Public REITs trade on stock exchanges with daily liquidity but exhibit higher correlation to equity markets. Private REITs (non-traded REITs) own similar assets but don't trade publicly, offering real estate exposure without daily price volatility.

Private REITs typically allow redemptions quarterly or annually subject to limitations. They've grown popular for investors seeking real estate income without full illiquidity.

Separate Accounts and Joint Ventures

Larger investors may establish separate accounts with managers to purchase specific properties or portfolios, providing full transparency and control but requiring larger minimums (typically $10M+).

Joint ventures involve partnering directly with developers or operators on specific projects, offering higher potential returns but concentrating risk in single assets.

Property Sectors and Strategies

Real estate encompasses diverse property types with different risk-return characteristics:

Multifamily (Apartments)

Residential rental properties ranging from garden-style complexes to high-rise urban towers. Multifamily benefits from:

Strong, consistent demand—everyone needs housing

Short lease terms (typically 12 months) allowing rent adjustments

Demographic tailwinds from household formation

Resilience during economic cycles

Office

Workspace for companies, from single-tenant suburban buildings to downtown towers. Office faces structural headwinds from remote work but quality properties in strong markets remain valuable. Key factors include:

Location quality and access to talent pools

Building class (A, B, C) and amenities

Lease duration (often 5-10 years) providing income stability

Tenant creditworthiness and industry diversification

Industrial and Logistics

Warehouses, distribution centers, and last-mile delivery facilities. E-commerce growth has driven exceptional industrial performance. Characteristics include:

Long-term leases (5-10 years) with creditworthy tenants

Lower capital expenditure requirements than office

Supply constraints in key logistics markets

Strong rental growth in infill locations

Retail

Shopping centers, malls, and street retail. Retail has undergone significant disruption from e-commerce but well-located, experiential retail (grocery-anchored, entertainment) remains resilient:

Grocery-anchored centers provide necessity-based traffic

Open-air lifestyle centers adapt to consumer preferences

Single-tenant net lease (Walgreens, Starbucks) offers bond-like income

Regional malls face structural challenges

Hospitality

Hotels and resorts generate revenue from nightly stays. Hospitality offers high operational leverage—income fluctuates significantly with economic cycles:

Limited-service (Hampton Inn, Courtyard) versus full-service (Marriott, Hilton)

Business versus leisure orientation

Brand affiliation versus independent

Higher risk-return profile than other property types

Specialty Sectors

Niche property types serving specific uses:

Self-storage: Recession-resistant with low capital intensity

Healthcare: Medical office buildings, senior housing, skilled nursing

Data centers: Technology infrastructure with long-term tenant commitments

Life sciences: Lab space for biotech and pharmaceutical research

Student housing: Near universities with predictable enrollment

Risk-Return Strategies

Real estate funds pursue different strategies along the risk-return spectrum:

Core

Stabilized, high-quality properties in strong markets with creditworthy tenants. Core strategies target 6-9% total returns through:

Current income (4-6% yields)

Modest appreciation (2-3% annually)

Minimal leverage (0-40% loan-to-value)

Low volatility

Core suits investors prioritizing current income and capital preservation.

Core-Plus

Properties requiring light value-add work or located in secondary markets. Core-plus targets 8-11% returns through:

Property improvements (renovations, repositioning)

Lease-up of vacant space

Moderate leverage (40-60% LTV)

Higher income and appreciation than core

Value-Add

Properties requiring significant improvements, repositioning, or lease-up. Value-add targets 11-15% returns through:

Major renovations or redevelopment

Operational improvements

Leasing of significantly vacant buildings

Moderate to high leverage (50-70% LTV)

Value-add involves higher risk but offers appreciation potential similar to private equity.

Opportunistic

Development, ground-up construction, or distressed properties. Opportunistic targets 15%+ returns through:

New construction and development

Significant repositioning or conversion

Distressed or foreclosed properties

High leverage (60-80% LTV)

Opportunistic strategies carry the highest risk with minimal current income during development or stabilization periods.

Due Diligence: What to Evaluate

Market Fundamentals

Real estate is location-dependent. Assess macro and micro market factors:

Supply-Demand Dynamics: Job growth, population trends, new construction pipeline

Economic Drivers: Employment diversity, income levels, corporate headquarters

Barriers to Entry: Land constraints, zoning restrictions limiting new supply

Submarket Quality: Specific neighborhoods within metro areas vary significantly

Strong markets have diversified economies, population growth, and supply constraints.

Property-Level Analysis

Evaluate individual asset quality:

Physical Condition: Building age, recent capital improvements, deferred maintenance

Location: Accessibility, visibility, nearby amenities, competitive properties

Tenant Quality: Credit ratings, industry health, lease expiration schedule

Occupancy and Lease Terms: Current occupancy, lease duration, renewal probability

Operating Expenses: Historical expense ratios, controllable vs. uncontrollable costs

Manager Capabilities

Real estate requires active management. Assess:

Acquisition Discipline: Underwriting conservatism, ability to source off-market deals

Asset Management: Leasing expertise, tenant relationships, property operations

Value Creation Track Record: Successful renovations, repositionings, developments

Local Market Presence: On-the-ground teams in target markets

Disposition Timing: Historical ability to sell properties at optimal times

Financial Analysis

Review deal-level economics:

Purchase Price: Valuation relative to replacement cost and comparable sales

Going-In Cap Rate: Net operating income divided by purchase price

Pro Forma Returns: Projected cash flows and exit assumptions

Leverage Terms: Loan amount, interest rate, maturity, recourse vs. non-recourse

Sensitivity Analysis: How returns change with varying rent growth, exit cap rates, holding periods

Fee Structures and Economics

Real estate fee structures vary by vehicle type:

Fund Management Fees

Closed-end funds typically charge 1.0-1.5% annually on committed capital or invested capital, depending on fund stage. Open-end funds may charge fees on NAV.

Some funds calculate fees on gross asset value (property values) while others use net equity (after debt), creating different effective fee rates.

Performance Fees (Promote)

Real estate funds typically structure promotes as:

Carried interest: 10-20% of profits above an 8% preferred return

Promotes may be deal-by-deal or fund-level (better for LPs)

Some funds use tiered promotes—higher carry percentages at higher return levels

Property-Level Fees

Managers may charge:

Acquisition fees: 0.5-1.5% of purchase price

Asset management fees: 0.5-1.0% of property revenue

Disposition fees: 0.5-1.5% of sale price

Construction management fees: 3-5% of construction costs

Quality managers often waive or offset property-level fees against fund management fees.

Fee Alignment

Look for GP commitments of 2-5% of fund equity, ensuring managers invest personal capital. Clawback provisions allow recovery of excess promotes if later investments underperform.

Risk Factors and Considerations

Market Cycle Risk

Real estate is cyclical. Buying at peak valuations during low cap rate environments can result in disappointing returns if exit cap rates expand. Vintage year matters significantly.

Mitigate through:

Disciplined underwriting with conservative exit assumptions

Focus on income generation rather than appreciation

Vintage year diversification across multiple fund commitments

Leverage Risk

Most real estate investments use 40-70% debt financing. Leverage amplifies both gains and losses. Floating rate debt exposes properties to interest rate risk if rates rise and properties cannot pass through costs via rent increases.

Assess:

Loan-to-value ratios and debt service coverage

Fixed vs. floating rate debt

Loan maturity dates relative to expected hold periods

Recourse vs. non-recourse structures

Tenant and Lease Risk

Properties depend on tenants paying rent. Tenant bankruptcies, non-renewals, or downsizing reduce income and require costly re-leasing efforts.

Evaluate:

Tenant credit quality and financial health

Lease expiration concentration

Market rental rates versus in-place rents

Re-leasing costs (tenant improvements, leasing commissions)

Liquidity Risk

Private real estate is illiquid. Funds have fixed lives or limited redemption windows. Property sales take months and may require discounts in distressed markets.

Only invest capital clients can commit for 5-10 years.

Regulatory and Zoning Risk

Properties operate under local regulations. Zoning changes, rent control (for multifamily), environmental requirements, or property tax reassessments can impact economics.

Geographic Concentration

Some funds concentrate in single metros or regions. While this allows deep local expertise, it increases exposure to market-specific risks. Diversified funds spread investments across 15-30 markets.

Tax Implications

Real estate offers attractive tax characteristics:

Depreciation Benefits

Buildings generate annual depreciation deductions (typically over 27.5-39 years), sheltering cash distributions from current taxation. Investors may receive distributions exceeding reported taxable income.

Cost Segregation

Managers may use cost segregation studies to accelerate depreciation on property components, increasing near-term deductions and deferring taxes.

1031 Exchanges

At the fund level, some managers use 1031 exchanges to sell properties and reinvest proceeds into new properties without triggering capital gains, deferring taxes.

Depreciation Recapture

Upon sale, depreciation claimed is recaptured as ordinary income (up to 25%) rather than capital gains. Remaining gains receive capital gains treatment.

Opportunity Zones

Investments in Qualified Opportunity Zones can defer and potentially eliminate capital gains taxes on appreciated positions reinvested into these designated low-income areas.

K-1 Reporting

Real estate partnerships issue K-1 forms showing income, deductions, and gains. K-1s may include multi-state source income, requiring filing in multiple jurisdictions.

Client Suitability Assessment

Real estate suits clients seeking:

Current Income

Real estate typically distributes 3-6% annual yields from rental income, appealing to income-focused investors, especially in low-interest-rate environments.

Inflation Protection

Property values and rents tend to rise with inflation. Multifamily with short leases adjusts rents annually. Other property types incorporate rent escalators.

Diversification

Real estate exhibits low correlation to stocks and bonds, providing diversification benefits in balanced portfolios.

Tangible Asset Exposure

Some clients appreciate real estate's physical nature and ability to understand value creation through property improvements.

Moderate Liquidity Tolerance

Unlike PE's 10-year lockups, real estate funds often begin distributing income within 1-2 years and fully liquidate within 7-10 years. Open-end funds offer even more liquidity.

Portfolio Construction Considerations

Strategic Allocation

Institutional investors typically allocate 5-15% to private real estate. Individual investors might target 5-10% depending on income needs and liquidity constraints.

Real estate complements both equities (appreciation potential) and bonds (income generation) while providing inflation protection.

Strategy Diversification

Blend strategies across the risk spectrum:

Core for stable income (50-60% of allocation)

Core-plus and value-add for enhanced returns (30-40%)

Opportunistic sparingly for appreciation potential (10-20%)

Match strategy mix to risk tolerance.

Property Type Diversification

Spread across property sectors:

Multifamily: 25-35% (stable demand, demographic tailwinds)

Industrial: 20-30% (e-commerce growth, supply constraints)

Office: 10-20% (higher risk but potential value in quality assets)

Retail: 5-15% (selective exposure to grocery-anchored, necessity-based)

Specialty: 10-20% (self-storage, healthcare, data centers)

Geographic Diversification

Invest across multiple metros to reduce market-specific risk. Blend:

Gateway markets (NYC, SF, LA) for stability and liquidity

Sunbelt growth markets (Austin, Nashville, Phoenix) for appreciation

Secondary markets for value and yields

Vintage Year Diversification

Commit to new real estate funds over multiple years to diversify entry points across market cycles, avoiding concentrated exposure to peak valuations.

Performance Benchmarking

Relevant Benchmarks

Compare private real estate to:

NCREIF Fund Index (ODCE): Open-end core funds

NCREIF Property Index (NPI): Direct property returns

Cambridge Associates Real Estate Index: All strategies

FTSE NAREIT Index: Public REITs (understanding correlation differences)

Key Metrics

Equity Multiple: Total distributions divided by invested capital

Quality real estate funds deliver:

Core: 6-9% total returns, 1.3-1.5x equity multiples

Key Takeaways for Advisors

Private real estate provides portfolio diversification, current income, and inflation protection through ownership of tangible assets generating cash flow from rents.

Success requires matching investment strategies to client objectives—core for income and stability, value-add and opportunistic for appreciation. Understanding property sectors, market dynamics, and manager capabilities drives investment selection.

Real estate's combination of bond-like income and equity-like appreciation, enhanced by favorable tax treatment, makes it a core alternative asset class for diversified portfolios. For clients with appropriate time horizons and income needs, private real estate deserves meaningful portfolio allocations.

What Infrastructure Investing Is and Why It Matters

Infrastructure investments involve ownership stakes in essential physical assets and systems that societies depend on daily—power plants, toll roads, airports, water systems, telecommunications networks, and energy pipelines.

These aren't speculative growth investments. Infrastructure assets generate predictable cash flows from long-term contracts or regulated revenue streams. A toll road collects fees from drivers. A power plant sells electricity under 20-year contracts. A cell tower leases space to wireless carriers on multi-year agreements.

For investors, infrastructure offers three primary benefits: steady income streams, inflation protection (many contracts include inflation escalators), and low correlation to traditional stocks and bonds. During market volatility, infrastructure assets continue operating and generating cash flow.

For advisors, infrastructure provides portfolio diversification and income generation for clients seeking alternatives to bonds in a low-yield environment.

Investment Structures and Vehicles

Closed-End Infrastructure Funds

Similar to private equity, most infrastructure funds operate as closed-end limited partnerships. Investors commit capital that's called over 4-6 years as assets are acquired. Fund lives typically span 10-15 years.

These funds acquire multiple infrastructure assets, manage them for income and value appreciation, then exit through sales to other infrastructure investors or strategic buyers.

Open-End/Perpetual Funds

Some managers offer open-end funds with periodic subscription and redemption windows (quarterly or annually). These funds hold diversified portfolios of mature infrastructure assets and provide more liquidity than closed-end structures, though with limitations on redemption amounts.

Listed Infrastructure Funds

Publicly traded infrastructure funds invest in infrastructure companies and assets while offering daily liquidity. They provide easier access but trade at market prices that may disconnect from underlying asset values during volatility.

Direct/Co-Investment Opportunities

Larger investors may access direct ownership stakes in specific infrastructure projects alongside fund sponsors, reducing fees but requiring larger capital commitments and concentration risk.

Infrastructure Sectors and Asset Types

Infrastructure investments span multiple sectors with different risk-return profiles:

Transportation

Toll roads and bridges: Revenue from usage fees, often with inflation-linked toll increases

Airports: Landing fees, terminal leases, parking, and retail concessions

Ports and marine terminals: Cargo handling fees tied to trade volumes

Rail infrastructure: Track ownership, freight terminals, passenger rail systems

Transportation assets typically have long useful lives (50+ years) and stable demand driven by economic activity.

Utilities and Energy

Electric transmission and distribution: Regulated utilities with stable rate structures

Natural gas pipelines and storage: Transportation fees under long-term contracts

Water and wastewater systems: Essential services with municipal contracts

Renewable energy: Solar and wind farms with power purchase agreements

Utility assets offer highly predictable cash flows under regulatory frameworks or long-term contracts.

Communications

Cell towers: Lease revenue from wireless carriers on 10-20 year contracts

Fiber optic networks: Data transmission capacity leased to telecom and internet providers

Data centers: Facilities housing servers and computing infrastructure

Communications infrastructure benefits from growing data consumption and 5G network buildout.

Social Infrastructure

Healthcare facilities: Hospitals and medical office buildings with long-term leases

Educational facilities: Student housing, schools under public-private partnerships

Government buildings: Courthouses, administrative facilities with government tenants

Social infrastructure provides stable, often government-backed revenue streams.

Energy Transition

Renewable generation: Wind, solar, hydroelectric, and battery storage

Electric vehicle charging networks: Emerging infrastructure for transportation electrification

Hydrogen production and distribution: Developing clean energy infrastructure

Energy transition assets offer growth exposure while supporting decarbonization goals.

Due Diligence: What to Evaluate

Asset Quality and Competitive Position

Assess the fundamental strength of underlying assets:

Essentiality: How critical is this infrastructure to users? Assets with no substitutes (the only bridge across a river) have stronger pricing power than those with alternatives.

Barriers to Entry: Regulatory licenses, high capital costs, and long development timelines protect existing assets from competition.

Remaining Useful Life: Assets with 30+ years of useful life require less near-term capital investment.

Location Quality: Toll roads in growing metropolitan areas generate more traffic than rural routes.

Revenue Predictability

Understand how revenue is generated and protected:

Contract Structure: Long-term contracts (10-30 years) with creditworthy counterparties provide stability. Review contract terms, renewal provisions, and termination rights.

Inflation Protection: Many infrastructure contracts include CPI escalators or formula-based rate increases, protecting purchasing power.

Volume Risk: Some assets bear traffic/volume risk (toll roads), while others have availability-based revenue (power plants paid to be available regardless of dispatch).

Regulatory Framework: Regulated utilities typically earn allowed returns on capital invested. Understand the regulatory jurisdiction and history of rate decisions.

Operational Track Record

Infrastructure requires active asset management:

Operating History: Review historical availability, capacity utilization, and operating costs.

Maintenance and Capital Expenditure: Understand ongoing maintenance needs and planned capital improvements.

Operating Partner: Many funds partner with specialized operators (airport managers, utility operators). Assess their experience and incentive alignment.

Environmental Compliance: Infrastructure assets must meet environmental regulations. Review compliance history and potential future requirements.

Manager Expertise

Infrastructure investing requires specialized knowledge:

Sector Experience: Managers should have deep expertise in target sectors—power generation requires different skills than transportation.

Deal Sourcing: Strong managers have networks providing proprietary deal flow and avoiding competitive auctions.

Asset Management Capabilities: Post-acquisition value creation through operational improvements, contract renegotiations, and strategic expansions.

ESG Integration: Infrastructure assets have environmental and social impacts. Leading managers integrate ESG considerations into investment decisions and asset management.

Fee Structures and Economics

Infrastructure fund fees typically align with asset characteristics:

Management Fees

Most infrastructure funds charge 1.0-1.5% annually on committed capital during the investment period, then on invested capital or NAV thereafter. This is lower than PE's typical 2% due to infrastructure's lower-risk profile and operational nature.

Performance Fees

Carried interest typically ranges from 10-20% of profits above an 8% hurdle rate. The lower carry relative to PE (which typically uses 20%) reflects infrastructure's focus on steady income rather than aggressive appreciation.

Transaction Fees

Like PE, infrastructure managers may charge portfolio companies acquisition, financing, or advisory fees. Quality managers offset some or all of these fees against management fees owed by limited partners.

Alignment Mechanisms

Look for GP commitments of 2-5% of fund size, ensuring managers have personal capital invested alongside LPs. Clawback provisions protect LPs by allowing recovery of excess carry if later investments underperform.

Risk Factors and Considerations

Regulatory and Political Risk

Infrastructure operates in regulated environments. Changes to regulatory frameworks, rate-setting methodologies, or political priorities can impact returns. Nationalization, while rare in developed markets, remains possible.

Mitigate this through:

Geographic diversification across regulatory jurisdictions

Focus on essential services less subject to political interference

Investment in stable regulatory regimes with rule-of-law protections

Operational Risk

Infrastructure assets require ongoing operations and maintenance. Poor management, deferred maintenance, or operational failures can damage assets and reduce cash flows.

Look for:

Experienced operators with proven track records

Adequate capital reserves for maintenance

Insurance coverage for operational risks

Technology and Obsolescence Risk

Some infrastructure faces disruption from new technologies. Toll roads compete with autonomous vehicles and remote work. Fossil fuel infrastructure faces transition risk from renewables.

Assess:

Remaining useful life relative to technology evolution timelines

Asset flexibility to adapt to changing technology

Contractual protections against volume declines

Interest Rate Sensitivity

Infrastructure valuations incorporate long-term cash flow projections discounted to present value. Rising interest rates increase discount rates, potentially reducing valuations.

However, many infrastructure assets have inflation-linked revenues that partially offset rate increases. Avoid overpaying during low-rate environments.

Liquidity and Exit Risk

Infrastructure investments typically require 10-15 year commitments. Secondary markets exist but may offer limited liquidity at discounted prices. Ensure clients can tolerate extended holding periods.

Concentration Risk

Some funds concentrate in single assets or sectors. While this can enhance returns, it increases exposure to asset-specific risks. Diversified funds holding 15-30 assets across sectors provide more stability.

Tax Implications

Infrastructure investments have unique tax characteristics:

Depreciation Benefits

Infrastructure assets generate significant depreciation deductions, offsetting taxable income. This can result in tax-deferred cash distributions where investors receive cash flow but report lower taxable income.

K-1 Complexity

Infrastructure funds issue K-1 forms reflecting pass-through income, deductions, and credits. K-1s may include:

Ordinary income from operations

Capital gains from asset sales

Depreciation and interest deductions

State-source income requiring multi-state tax filings

Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI)

Tax-exempt investors (IRAs, foundations) may face UBTI if infrastructure funds use debt financing, creating unexpected tax obligations.

Recapture Provisions

When assets are sold, previously claimed depreciation may be recaptured as ordinary income rather than capital gains, affecting after-tax returns.

Renewable Energy Credits

Investments in renewable infrastructure may generate tax credits (Investment Tax Credits, Production Tax Credits) that pass through to investors, enhancing after-tax returns.

Client Suitability Assessment

Infrastructure suits specific client profiles:

Income-Focused Investors

Clients seeking current income benefit from infrastructure's steady distributions. Target yields typically range from 5-9% depending on risk profile.

Inflation-Concerned Clients

Those worried about purchasing power erosion appreciate infrastructure's inflation-protected revenue streams.

Long-Term Patient Capital

Clients must accept 10-15 year lockups. Those needing liquidity within five years should avoid traditional infrastructure funds.

Risk-Adjusted Return Seekers

Infrastructure typically targets 8-12% net returns—lower than PE but higher than bonds, with less volatility than both PE and public equities.

ESG-Conscious Investors

Infrastructure enables impact investing through renewable energy, public transportation, and water systems that deliver both returns and societal benefits.

Portfolio Construction Considerations

Strategic Allocation

Institutions typically allocate 5-15% to infrastructure. Individual investors might target 3-10% depending on income needs and liquidity constraints.

Infrastructure sits between equities and bonds in risk-return profiles, providing:

Higher yields than bonds

Lower volatility than equities

Inflation protection

Diversification benefits

Core vs. Core-Plus vs. Value-Add Strategies

Different infrastructure strategies offer varying risk-return profiles:

Core Infrastructure: Mature, operational assets with long-term contracts or stable regulatory frameworks. Lower risk, yields of 5-8%, minimal leverage.

Core-Plus: Similar to core but may include some development risk, shorter contracts, or moderate leverage. Yields of 7-10%.

Value-Add/Opportunistic: Development projects, distressed assets, or higher-risk sectors. Targets returns of 12-15%+ with higher volatility.

Match strategy to client risk tolerance and return requirements.

Geographic Diversification

Spread investments across:

Developed markets (OECD countries) for stability

Emerging markets for higher growth and yields

Different regulatory regimes to reduce jurisdiction-specific risk

Sector Diversification

Blend defensive sectors (utilities, water) with growth-oriented sectors (renewable energy, data infrastructure) to balance stability and appreciation potential.

Performance Benchmarking

Relevant Benchmarks

Compare infrastructure investments to:

Cambridge Associates Infrastructure Index

MSCI Global Infrastructure Index (for listed infrastructure)

Custom benchmarks blending bonds and equities (e.g., 60/40 mix)

Key Metrics

Current Yield: Annual distributions divided by invested capital

IRR: Time-weighted return including income and appreciation

Cash Yield: Cash distributions net of return of capital

MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital): Total value divided by invested capital

Quality infrastructure funds deliver:

Consistent quarterly distributions

Net IRRs of 8-12% depending on strategy

Low volatility in NAV

Inflation-matching or inflation-beating returns

Key Takeaways for Advisors

Infrastructure provides unique portfolio benefits through steady income, inflation protection, and diversification. Unlike growth-oriented private equity, infrastructure emphasizes current cash flow from essential assets.

Success requires understanding different infrastructure sectors, assessing asset quality and revenue stability, and matching investment strategies to client needs. Core infrastructure suits income-focused, conservative clients. Value-add strategies fit those seeking higher returns with more risk.

Infrastructure's combination of bond-like income and equity-like appreciation makes it particularly valuable in today's environment of low bond yields and equity market uncertainty. For clients with appropriate time horizons and income objectives, infrastructure deserves consideration as a portfolio diversifier and income generator.

Making Your First Registered Fund Investment

A complete guide to your first registered alternative fund

Making Your First Registered Fund Investment

Registered alternative funds provide access to private equity, credit, real estate, and infrastructure strategies through SEC-regulated structures with improved liquidity and lower minimums than traditional private funds. While more accessible than their private fund counterparts, registered alternatives still require thorough due diligence and understanding before investing.

Here's how to approach your first registered fund investment with confidence.

Understanding What You're Buying

Before committing capital, understand the fundamental structure and strategy.

Fund Structure

Determine which registered structure you're considering:

Interval Funds offer quarterly or semi-annual redemption windows for 5-25% of fund assets. You can request redemptions during these windows, though the fund may prorate if total requests exceed limits. Between windows, shares are illiquid.

BDCs trade on exchanges with daily liquidity at market prices. You buy and sell shares like stocks, though prices may trade at premiums or discounts to net asset value.

Tender Offer Funds periodically offer to repurchase shares when the manager determines liquidity is available. Timing is less predictable than interval funds.

Closed-End Funds trade on exchanges after an initial offering. You purchase shares at market prices rather than NAV.

Understanding the structure clarifies liquidity expectations, pricing mechanisms, and how you'll ultimately exit the investment.

Investment Strategy

Registered funds employ various alternative strategies:

Private credit: Lending to middle-market companies

Real estate: Property ownership or real estate debt

Infrastructure: Essential assets like power, transportation, utilities

Private equity: Growth capital or buyout investments in private companies

Multi-strategy: Diversified approaches across alternatives

Each strategy has different risk-return profiles, income characteristics, and market cycle sensitivities. Understand what you're investing in before committing capital.

The Key Documents to Review

Unlike private funds with limited documentation, registered funds provide extensive disclosure. Use these resources:

The Prospectus

This is your primary resource. Every registered fund publishes a prospectus describing:

Investment objectives and strategies

Principal risks

Fee structures and expense ratios

Performance history (if available)

Management team and fund governance

Purchase and redemption procedures

Tax considerations

Read it completely. Don't skim. The prospectus contains everything you need to evaluate the opportunity.

Pay particular attention to the "Principal Risks" section. These aren't boilerplate—they describe actual risks specific to this fund's strategy.

Statement of Additional Information (SAI)

The SAI provides expanded details beyond the prospectus:

Detailed portfolio manager biographies

Complete fee schedules and arrangements

Financial statements

Voting and governance rights

Distribution policies

While lengthier and more technical than the prospectus, the SAI offers valuable insights for thorough due diligence.

Annual and Semi-Annual Reports

If the fund has operating history, review its most recent annual and semi-annual reports showing:

Complete portfolio holdings

Performance results and NAV changes

Financial statements

Management commentary on strategy and outlook

These reports reveal what the fund actually owns and how it has performed, providing concrete evidence beyond prospectus promises.

Fund Fact Sheet

Most funds publish monthly or quarterly fact sheets summarizing:

Performance returns

Top holdings

Portfolio characteristics (average yield, duration, credit quality)

Strategy overview

Fact sheets provide quick snapshots but don't substitute for prospectus review.

Understanding Fees and Expenses

Registered fund fees are disclosed differently than private funds but involve similar economic costs.

Management Fees

The base fee for portfolio management, typically 1.5-2.5% annually for alternative strategies. This covers investment research, portfolio management, and operational overhead.

Performance-Based Fees

Many alternative funds charge incentive fees or performance allocations above hurdle rates. A fund might charge 15-20% of returns above an 8% hurdle, similar to private fund carried interest.

These fees align manager and investor interests but significantly impact net returns when performance exceeds the hurdle.

Other Operating Expenses

Additional costs include:

Administration and accounting fees

Legal and audit fees

Custody and transfer agent fees

Board of trustees/directors fees

Registration and filing fees

Total Expense Ratio

The prospectus discloses a total expense ratio summarizing all annual costs as a percentage of fund assets. For alternative interval funds, total expense ratios typically range from 2.5-4.5% before performance fees.

Compare this to private funds charging 1.5-2% management fees—the all-in costs may be similar once you account for private fund administrative expenses.

Performance Fee Example

Understanding how performance fees work in practice helps you evaluate total costs:

A fund charges 2% management fees plus 20% of returns above an 8% hurdle. If the fund returns 12% gross:

Gross return: 12%

Hurdle: 8%

Excess return: 4%

Performance fee: 20% × 4% = 0.8%

Management fee: 2%

Total fees: 2.8%

Net return: 12% - 2.8% = 9.2%

If the fund returns only 8% gross, you pay just the 2% management fee, netting 6%.

Evaluating the Investment Manager

The manager drives performance. Thorough manager due diligence is essential:

Track Record and Performance

Review historical returns across market cycles:

Net returns after all fees

Performance versus benchmarks

Consistency across different market environments

Performance during stress periods (2020 COVID, 2008 financial crisis)

Be cautious of funds with limited history or those cherry-picking timeframes. Three-plus years of live performance provides meaningful evidence, though longer is better.

Investment Team

Assess the people managing your capital:

Years of experience in the strategy

Prior roles and performance track records

Team stability and retention

Depth of resources (research analysts, operations, compliance)

Strong teams have continuity, complementary skills, and proven experience managing through cycles.

Investment Process

Understand how the manager sources, evaluates, and manages investments:

Deal sourcing and origination

Underwriting and due diligence approach

Risk management framework

Portfolio construction and diversification

Monitoring and workout capabilities

Disciplined, repeatable processes produce more consistent results than one-off successes or market timing.

Organizational Stability

Consider the broader organization:

Assets under management and growth trajectory

Ownership structure and incentives

Regulatory history and compliance record

Conflicts of interest and how they're managed

Well-established organizations with strong compliance cultures and aligned ownership provide stability.

ESG Integration

If environmental, social, and governance factors matter to your clients, understand how the manager incorporates ESG into investment decisions and portfolio management.

Assessing Portfolio Characteristics

Review what the fund actually owns or intends to own:

Diversification

Strong funds diversify across:

Number of holdings (30-100+ positions for credit and real estate)

Industry sectors

Geographic regions

Investment sizes

Concentrated funds (10-20 holdings) carry more individual position risk, requiring higher conviction in manager stock-picking.

Credit Quality or Asset Quality

For credit funds, review:

Average credit ratings

Percentage of secured vs. unsecured loans

Debt-to-EBITDA ratios of borrowers

Industries and end-market exposure

For real estate funds, assess:

Property types and locations

Occupancy rates and lease terms

Tenant credit quality

Leverage levels

Higher quality generally means lower yields but more stability.

Liquidity Profile

Understand the underlying portfolio's liquidity:

Can assets be sold within months if needed?

Are there active secondary markets for holdings?

What percentage might be difficult to sell quickly?

This helps you assess whether the fund's redemption terms match its portfolio liquidity.

Understanding Risks

Every investment involves risks. Alternative strategies carry specific considerations:

Illiquidity Risk

Even interval funds offering quarterly redemptions may prorate or suspend redemptions if requests exceed limits. During market stress, you may not be able to access capital when needed.

Valuation Risk

Illiquid holdings require subjective valuations. While registered funds follow standardized procedures with board oversight, NAVs for private assets involve estimates that may not reflect true market values.

Market Risk

Alternative assets aren't immune to market downturns. Private credit faces defaults during recessions. Real estate values decline in economic stress. Understand how different market environments might affect returns.

Leverage Risk

Many funds use leverage to enhance returns. Borrowing amplifies gains but also magnifies losses and creates refinancing risk if credit markets tighten.

Concentration Risk

Sector-focused funds (technology lending, apartment buildings) face concentrated exposure to specific market segments.

Manager Risk

Your returns depend entirely on manager skill. Poor decision-making, operational failures, or key person departures can damage performance.

The prospectus's "Principal Risks" section details specific risks for each fund. Read this section carefully and ensure you're comfortable with the risks described.

Tax Considerations

Understanding tax implications before investing avoids surprises:

1099 vs. K-1 Reporting

Registered funds structured as corporations (most BDCs) issue 1099-DIV forms reporting dividends. This simplifies tax filing.

Funds structured as partnerships issue K-1 forms showing allocated income, gains, and deductions. K-1s arrive later (March/April) and may create multi-state filing requirements.

The prospectus specifies which tax form the fund issues.

Income Characterization

Most alternative fund income is taxed as ordinary income, not qualified dividends, resulting in higher tax rates.

Some funds generate long-term capital gains receiving preferential tax treatment. Review the fund's historical income composition in annual reports.

Tax-Deferred Accounts

Alternative funds work well in IRAs or other tax-deferred accounts, sheltering ordinary income from current taxation.

Be aware of Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI) if funds use significant leverage, which can create unexpected tax obligations in tax-exempt accounts.

Return of Capital

Some funds distribute return of capital—giving back your own money rather than investment earnings. While not immediately taxable, this reduces your cost basis, potentially creating larger future capital gains.

Making the Investment Decision

After thorough research, determine whether the investment aligns with client needs:

Does the Strategy Fit?

Align the investment strategy with portfolio objectives:

Income generation: Private credit or real estate debt

Diversification: Multi-strategy or infrastructure

Growth: Private equity or real estate equity

Are Fees Reasonable?

Compare total expense ratios and performance fees to peers. While all alternative funds charge more than index funds, fees should be competitive within the strategy.

Is the Manager Proven?

Select managers with demonstrated track records, experienced teams, and disciplined processes.

Can You Accept the Liquidity Terms?

Ensure clients can tolerate quarterly redemption windows, potential proration, or trading at premiums/discounts for BDCs.

Is the Minimum Investment Appropriate?

Confirm the minimum ($2,500-$25,000 typically) fits within overall alternative allocation targets without creating over-concentration.

The Investment Process

Once you've decided to proceed:

Account Setup

Work with the fund company or distributor to establish accounts. You'll need:

Account application

Subscription agreement

Beneficiary designations

Tax forms (W-9)

Purchase Execution

Interval Funds: Submit subscription orders by specified deadlines (often month-end). Fund shares are priced at month-end NAV, with settlement typically within 5-7 days.

BDCs/Closed-End Funds: Place buy orders through your broker like any stock purchase.

Confirmation and Documentation

After purchase, you'll receive:

Trade confirmation

Account statement

Access to fund documents and reports

Information on distribution payments

Ongoing Monitoring

Track your investment through:

Quarterly statements

NAV updates (monthly or quarterly depending on fund type)

Distribution payments

Annual and semi-annual reports

Updated prospectuses

Best Practices for First-Time Investors

Start with Core Strategies

Begin with lower-risk core strategies (senior lending, core real estate) before moving to opportunistic approaches. Build conviction gradually.

Diversify Across Managers

Don't concentrate all alternative exposure with one manager. Spread investments across 2-3 managers with different approaches.

Size Positions Appropriately

Alternative funds typically should comprise 5-15% of total portfolios for individual investors, depending on risk tolerance and liquidity needs.

Plan for Illiquidity

Only invest capital you can commit for the fund's expected holding period. Maintain adequate liquid reserves for unexpected needs.

Review Documentation Completely

Take time to read prospectuses and understand what you're buying. Don't rely on fact sheets or summaries alone.

Ask Questions

Contact fund companies with questions about strategies, risks, fees, or processes. Quality managers welcome investor inquiries.

Consider Professional Guidance

Work with advisors experienced in alternative investments to determine appropriate allocations, manager selection, and portfolio construction.

The Bottom Line

Registered alternative funds democratize access to institutional strategies through standardized, regulated structures with improved liquidity. While more accessible than private funds, they still require thorough due diligence focusing on strategy fit, manager quality, fee structures, and risk assessment.

Approach registered alternatives as you would any substantial investment—with careful research, realistic expectations, and alignment with overall financial objectives. The combination of alternative return streams, income generation, and regulatory protections makes registered funds valuable portfolio components for investors who understand what they're buying and can accept the liquidity constraints.

Your first registered fund investment begins a learning journey. Each opportunity you evaluate will deepen your understanding of alternative strategies and make you more sophisticated in assessing managers, structures, and portfolio fit.

Making Your First Pre-IPO Investment

How to approach pre-IPO opportunities with confidence

Pre-IPO investments offer premium return potential because they're illiquid and less transparent than public stocks. While public companies report quarterly and their shares trade instantly, private companies disclose less and your capital is locked up for years.

This creates both opportunity and risk. The opportunity: potentially higher returns than public markets. The risk: real potential for loss if you invest without understanding what you're buying.

Good pre-IPO investing happens when you understand the investment and do your homework. That might sound intimidating, but modern platforms provide the tools and information you need. Here's how to approach your first investment with confidence.

Understanding What You're Actually Buying

Before you commit capital, know what you're getting into. When you invest in a pre-IPO SPV, you're buying membership interests in a fund that owns shares of one specific private company. Your returns depend entirely on how that company performs.

If it grows and exits through an IPO or acquisition, you participate in those gains proportionally. If it struggles or fails, you could lose everything. This isn't like a diversified mutual fund where one bad position is offset by others—you're concentrated in a single company.

That makes research critical.

The Key Documents You'll See

The Offering Documents

These are your primary resources. It includes:

Investment terms: share class, price per share, valuation, minimums, and fees

Company overview: business model, products, customers, market opportunity

Management team and institutional investors

Company funding history

Risk factors specific to this investment

Read it. Don't skim it. The platform's research team compiled this specifically to help you evaluate the opportunity.

The Private Placement Memorandum (PPM)

This is the formal legal offering document. It describes the investment, its risks, and the terms. The PPM includes detailed risk disclosures—pay attention to the "Risk Factors" section.

Risks range from business-specific (competition, execution risk, market dynamics) to structural (illiquidity, company approval requirements, minority investor rights).

Sometimes, there are instead separate "Risk Factors" or "Key Terms" documents used to break out some information separately.

The Subscription Agreement

This is the binding contract that formalizes your investment. It outlines:

Your rights as an SPV member

Your proportional ownership

Distribution rights when the company exits

Your obligations and representations (like confirming you're an accredited investor)

Before this, you'll sign a non-binding term sheet that reserves your allocation. But once you sign the subscription agreement, you're committed.

Understanding Funding Rounds and Transactions

Private companies raise capital through funding rounds—Seed, Series A, Series B, Series C, and so on. Each round typically issues new shares at a higher valuation than the previous one, reflecting growth.

When you evaluate an investment, understand:

The funding history: How much capital has been raised? At what valuations? Who are the investors? This tells you whether the current price makes sense.

The share class: Different classes have different rights. Preferred shares (held by institutional investors) often have priority over common shares (held by employees and secondary buyers) in exits. The cap table shows these preferences clearly.

Funding round vs. secondary transaction: Funding rounds issue new shares directly from the company. Secondary transactions (most common on platforms) involve buying existing shares from current shareholders—often employees seeking liquidity. Secondary transactions don't capitalize the company, but they provide liquidity to existing shareholders.

Tender offers: Sometimes companies facilitate tender offers, allowing multiple shareholders to sell in a coordinated process at standardized, company-approved prices.

How to Do Your Research

Platforms provide extensive information, but supplement it with your own research to build conviction:

Review Company Communications

Visit the company's website. Read their blog, press releases, and newsroom. Understand their products, mission, and recent developments. Many private companies actively communicate their progress.

Follow Industry News

Search for recent articles, reports, and analysis about the company and its sector. Understanding the competitive landscape, market size, and trends helps you evaluate the company's positioning.

Use reputable business publications. Be skeptical of forums or unverified sources.

Use Platform Tools

Platforms provide research including company snapshots, valuation trends, market scores, and secondary market data. These tools aggregate information from thousands of transactions and users, giving unique insights into pricing and sentiment.

Assess Your Price

What are you paying relative to the company's last funding round? Are you at a premium or discount? Is that differential justified by performance since then?

Platforms typically establish pricing ranges to ensure offerings are fairly priced.

Independent Research

Read third-party research if available. Some platforms partner with research providers for deeper analysis on select companies.

Best Practices for First-Time Investors

Invest in Companies You Understand

Start with businesses whose products or business models you can readily comprehend. Familiarity gives you an advantage in evaluating potential.

Plan to Diversify

Don't put all your alternative allocation in one company. Build a portfolio of several pre-IPO investments across different sectors and stages. This reduces reliance on any single company's success.

Only Invest Capital You Can Lock Up

Pre-IPO investments require 2-10 year holding periods. Only invest money you won't need for other purposes during that timeframe.

Read Everything

Take time to read the offering materials, PPM, and subscription agreement completely. Understanding what you're agreeing to protects you and ensures informed decisions.

Ask Questions

Please ask any and all questions or refer to the SPV FAQ document(s). All questions are important to answer and understand. Especially as you are starting in the new landscape of alternative investments.

Consider Professional Advice

If you work with a financial advisor, discuss pre-IPO investments in context of your overall portfolio, goals, risk tolerance, and liquidity needs. They can help determine appropriate allocations.

The Bottom Line

Pre-IPO investing can add value to diversified portfolios, offering access to high-growth companies and potential for enhanced returns. Approach these opportunities with diligence, ask questions, and leverage available research tools.

Your first pre-IPO investment is the start of a learning journey. Each opportunity you evaluate will deepen your understanding of private markets and make you a more sophisticated investor.

Understanding Liquidity

How liquidity works across different fund structures

Understanding Liquidity in Registered Alternative Funds

One of the most significant advantages registered alternative funds offer over traditional private funds is improved liquidity. While private funds typically lock up capital for 7-10 years with no early exit options, registered alternatives provide various liquidity mechanisms—quarterly redemptions, daily trading, or periodic tender offers.

But liquidity in alternatives comes with important caveats and limitations. Understanding how different liquidity mechanisms work, their restrictions, and the potential challenges helps you set appropriate expectations and avoid surprises when you or your clients need to access capital.

Why Liquidity Matters Differently in Alternatives

Traditional mutual funds invest in liquid securities—stocks and bonds that can be sold within days. When investors redeem shares, fund managers simply sell enough securities to raise cash, process the redemption, and move on.

Alternative funds own illiquid assets—private company loans, commercial real estate, infrastructure projects—that can't be sold quickly without significant discounts. A private credit fund can't instantly sell a $10 million loan to a middle-market company. A real estate fund can't liquidate an office building in days.

This fundamental mismatch between investor liquidity expectations and asset liquidity creates challenges registered alternatives must manage carefully.

Interval Funds: Quarterly Redemption Windows

Interval funds represent the most common registered structure for illiquid alternative strategies. Understanding how their liquidity mechanism works is essential.

How Interval Fund Redemptions Work

Interval funds offer periodic repurchase offers, typically quarterly, for a limited percentage of outstanding shares (usually 5-25% of fund assets). Here's the process:

1. Repurchase Offer Announcement

The fund announces a repurchase offer 21-42 days before the repurchase pricing date. The announcement specifies:

The repurchase pricing date (when shares are valued)

The repurchase percentage (what portion of fund assets will be repurchased)

The tender deadline (when redemption requests must be submitted)

The expected payment date

For example: "The fund will offer to repurchase up to 5% of outstanding shares at NAV calculated on March 31, 2026. Tender requests must be received by March 25, 2026. Payment will be made by April 15, 2026."

2. Submitting Redemption Requests

Investors submit tender requests by the deadline, specifying:

How many shares they want to redeem

Whether the request is "all-or-none" (must be fully filled or canceled) or can be prorated

You don't know the exact redemption price when submitting your request since it's calculated on the future pricing date.

3. Proration

If total redemption requests exceed the repurchase percentage (say, 8% of fund assets when the fund offered 5%), the fund prorates all requests proportionally.

If you requested to redeem $10,000 and requests totaled 8% while the offer was for 5%, you'd receive approximately 62.5% of your request ($6,250), with the remainder staying invested.

4. Pricing and Settlement

The fund calculates NAV on the repurchase pricing date. Redemptions are processed at this NAV, with payments typically made within 7-14 days.

Key Limitations

Several factors constrain interval fund liquidity:

Repurchase Caps: The 5-25% quarterly limit means you may not be able to exit your full position in a single quarter. Complete liquidation might take 4-5 quarters even if redemptions aren't prorated.

Proration Risk: During market stress or fund performance issues, redemption requests often exceed limits, resulting in significant proration. You might receive only 25-50% of your requested redemption.

Suspension Authority: Funds can suspend repurchase offers entirely if:

Redemptions would force sales at disadvantageous prices

Market conditions make valuations unreliable

Regulatory requirements necessitate suspension

While rare, suspensions can trap capital when you most need liquidity.

Processing Time: Even successful redemptions take 2-3 weeks from tender deadline to payment receipt, unlike same-day settlement for stocks.

Business Development Companies: Daily Exchange Trading

BDCs offer daily liquidity through exchange trading, similar to stocks. You can buy or sell shares any trading day at market prices. This sounds ideal but comes with important considerations.

Trading at Market Prices vs. NAV

BDCs calculate NAV quarterly based on portfolio valuations, but shares trade at market prices determined by supply and demand.

Market prices frequently diverge from NAV:

Premiums: Shares trading above NAV (e.g., $11 per share when NAV is $10)

Discounts: Shares trading below NAV (e.g., $9 per share when NAV is $10)

Discounts and premiums fluctuate based on:

Market sentiment toward the BDC sector

Interest rate expectations

Credit market conditions

Company-specific factors (dividend sustainability, portfolio quality)

General equity market volatility

Implications for Investors

Trading at premiums or discounts creates economic consequences:

If you buy shares at a 10% premium ($11 when NAV is $10), you need the NAV to increase more than 10% just to break even if the premium disappears.

If shares trade at a 15% discount when you need to sell, you realize a 15% loss even if the underlying portfolio maintained its value.

Historical Volatility

During the March 2020 COVID crisis, many BDCs traded at 30-50% discounts to NAV as investors panic-sold. While NAVs declined 10-20% reflecting credit deterioration, market prices fell far more. Investors who sold at these discounts crystallized losses far exceeding underlying portfolio declines.

Conversely, during strong markets, BDCs often trade at 10-20% premiums, requiring new investors to pay above intrinsic value.

Benefits of Exchange Trading

Despite premium/discount volatility, daily liquidity offers advantages:

Immediate access to capital without waiting for quarterly windows

No proration—you can sell your entire position at once

Transparent real-time pricing

Ability to use limit orders and other trading strategies

BDC liquidity works best for investors who:

Can tolerate price volatility

Plan to hold long-term through premium/discount fluctuations

Don't need to access capital during market stress

Tender Offer Funds: Manager-Controlled Liquidity

Some registered funds use tender offers rather than regular interval repurchases. The fund announces when it will offer to repurchase shares, rather than maintaining a set quarterly schedule.

How Tender Offers Work

The manager decides when to offer liquidity based on:

Available cash from portfolio company exits or asset sales

Market conditions for selling portfolio holdings

Expected future cash needs

Investor demand for liquidity

Tender offers might occur quarterly, semi-annually, or opportunistically. Investors don't know when the next offer will occur until announced.

Advantages

Managers can align liquidity provision with portfolio cash generation

Avoids forced sales at disadvantageous times

More flexibility than fixed interval schedules

Disadvantages

Unpredictable timing creates planning challenges

Potential for extended periods without liquidity opportunities

Less transparency about future access to capital

Tender offer structures suit strategies with unpredictable cash flows (opportunistic private equity, development real estate) but require higher investor liquidity tolerance.

Closed-End Funds: Exchange Trading with No NAV Redemptions

Registered closed-end funds trade on exchanges like BDCs but don't offer NAV redemptions or repurchase programs. You can only exit by selling shares to other investors at market prices.

Like BDCs, closed-end funds frequently trade at significant premiums or discounts to NAV. Some closed-end funds implement managed distribution policies, share repurchase programs, or other mechanisms to reduce discount volatility, but premium/discount risk remains substantial.

Managing Liquidity in Your Portfolio

Understanding these liquidity mechanisms helps you structure alternatives allocations appropriately:

Match Liquidity to Client Needs

Conservative Liquidity Needs: Clients requiring access to capital within 1-2 years should avoid interval funds and focus on BDCs or closed-end funds with exchange liquidity, despite premium/discount risk.

Moderate Liquidity Needs: Those comfortable waiting 1-2 quarters for potential access can use interval funds, understanding proration risk.

Patient Capital: Clients with 3-5 year horizons can accept tender offer funds with less predictable liquidity timing.

Size Positions Appropriately

Don't allocate more to interval funds than you can afford to have locked up for 1-2 years. If a client has $100,000 in alternatives and needs liquidity within a year, interval funds should represent only a portion—perhaps $30,000-$50,000—with the remainder in more liquid vehicles.

Maintain Liquidity Reserves

Never put all investable assets into alternatives with limited liquidity. Maintain reserves in truly liquid investments (cash, money market funds, publicly traded bonds) to handle unexpected needs without forced alternative sales at disadvantageous times.

Plan for Proration

When modeling portfolio liquidity, assume 50% proration on interval fund redemptions during ordinary conditions and potentially 0% access during market stress. This conservative assumption prevents over-reliance on alternatives liquidity.

Understand Premium/Discount Dynamics

For BDCs and closed-end funds, track premium/discount history. Avoid buying at extreme premiums. Consider buying at discounts if you have conviction in underlying portfolio quality and can hold through volatility.

What Happens During Market Stress

Liquidity mechanisms face their greatest tests during market crises. Understanding stress scenarios helps you prepare:

Interval Fund Stress Scenarios

During significant market downturns or credit crises:

Redemption requests spike as investors seek cash

Proration increases significantly (you might receive only 20-30% of requested redemptions)

Boards may reduce repurchase percentages from 5% to 2-3% or suspend offers entirely

NAV declines may accompany reduced liquidity, creating double pain

The March 2020 COVID crisis saw some interval funds suspend repurchases while others continued with heavy proration. Investors unable to access capital faced difficult choices about whether to continue tendering or wait out the crisis.

BDC Stress Scenarios

Exchange-traded BDCs faced dramatic price declines during COVID:

Discounts to NAV widened to 30-50% as investors panicked

Daily liquidity remained available, but at severely depressed prices

Investors who sold at these discounts crystallized massive losses

Those who held through recovered most value as discounts narrowed

Daily liquidity proved to be a double-edged sword—available when needed but only at punishing prices.

Redemption Fees and Gates

Some funds impose additional liquidity constraints:

Redemption Fees

Funds may charge 1-2% redemption fees on shares held less than 1-2 years to discourage short-term trading and compensate long-term shareholders for liquidity provision costs.

These fees reduce the proceeds you receive from redemptions.

Gates

Some funds reserve the right to limit total redemptions to a percentage of fund assets over rolling periods (e.g., 10% annually). Gates provide additional protection against runs but further restrict liquidity.

Always review prospectus disclosure about redemption fees and gates before investing.

Tax Considerations in Redemptions

Redeeming interval fund shares or selling BDC shares creates taxable events:

Capital gains or losses based on your cost basis

Holding period determines short-term (ordinary income rates) vs. long-term treatment

Redemption proceeds arrive 2-3 weeks after tender, potentially creating cash flow timing issues if you need funds for tax payments

Plan redemptions with tax implications in mind, especially for December year-end redemptions that might push income into the current tax year.

Comparing Liquidity Across Structures

Understanding the trade-offs helps you select appropriate vehicles:

Setting Realistic Expectations

When discussing alternatives with clients, be honest about liquidity:

What Clients Should Expect:

Quarterly access opportunities (interval funds)

Potential proration reducing actual liquidity

Processing delays of 2-4 weeks from request to payment

Possibility of suspensions during extreme stress

Premium/discount volatility for exchange-traded vehicles

What Clients Should NOT Expect:

Same-day liquidity like bank accounts

Guaranteed access to full capital on demand

Stable redemption prices disconnected from market conditions

Liquidity during crises without economic cost

Managing expectations prevents panic redemptions and poor decision-making during stress periods.

Strategies for Optimizing Liquidity

Laddering

Invest in multiple interval funds with staggered repurchase dates, creating monthly or bi-monthly potential liquidity rather than quarterly.

Blending Structures

Combine interval funds (predictable but limited) with BDCs (volatile but always available) to balance liquidity characteristics.

Maintaining Dry Powder

Keep some alternative allocation uncommitted in liquid securities, allowing you to wait for optimal redemption timing rather than forced sales.

Strategic Redemptions

When you need partial liquidity, redeem from funds trading at premiums to NAV (for BDCs) or make small quarterly interval fund redemptions rather than large requests that ensure proration.

The Bottom Line

Liquidity in registered alternative funds represents a significant improvement over traditional private funds' multi-year lockups, but it's not equivalent to mutual fund or stock liquidity.

Interval funds offer predictable quarterly access with limits and proration risk. BDCs provide daily liquidity at volatile market prices disconnected from NAV. Tender offer funds give manager-controlled intermittent access. Each structure has trade-offs requiring careful consideration.

Success requires realistic expectations, appropriate position sizing, maintained liquidity reserves, and understanding that alternatives liquidity is most constrained exactly when you might most need it—during market stress.

For clients who understand these constraints and plan accordingly, registered alternatives' improved liquidity makes sophisticated institutional strategies accessible and manageable within diversified portfolios. The key is matching liquidity structures to client circumstances, avoiding over-allocation, and maintaining perspective that alternatives are long-term holdings occasionally requiring patience before accessing capital.

What Happens After You Invest

Monitoring, tax reporting, and ongoing management

Once you've completed your pre-IPO investment, a new phase begins: monitoring your investment, understanding your tax obligations, and being prepared for potential corporate actions or liquidity events. While pre-IPO investments are generally long-term, passive holdings, staying informed about your investment's status and understanding key concepts like NAV, K-1 tax forms, and potential exit scenarios will help you manage your portfolio effectively.

Monitoring Your Investment

After your investment closes, you'll receive confirmation via email and can access your investment summary and related documents through your portfolio page on the platform. From there, you can track the status and value of your holdings.

Understanding Net Asset Value (NAV): The Net Asset Value represents the estimated current value of your investment. For pre-IPO investments, calculating NAV involves more complexity than with public stocks because private company shares don't have real-time market prices. Instead, NAV is typically calculated using one of several methodologies:

Last Round Valuation: The most straightforward approach uses the price per share from the company's most recent funding round. If the company raised a Series D at $50 per share and your SPV holds 1,000 shares, the gross asset value would be $50,000.

Secondary Market Transactions: If there's active secondary market trading in the company's shares, recent transaction prices can inform NAV calculations. Platforms like Monark that facilitate ongoing secondary transactions can use this pricing data to establish reference values.

Third-Party Valuations: For some investments, independent valuation firms may be engaged to assess fair market value based on company financials, comparable company analysis, and industry trends.

Mark-to-Market Adjustments: When significant company events occur (new funding rounds, major customer wins or losses, market conditions, or industry developments), the NAV may be updated to reflect the new information.

It's important to understand that NAV is an estimate, not a guaranteed value. The actual value of your investment will only be realized when a liquidity event occurs—when the company goes public, gets acquired, or you're able to sell your shares in a secondary transaction. NAV calculations can help you track your investment's estimated performance, but they should be viewed as directional indicators rather than precise valuations.

Reviewing Performance: Most platforms provide regular updates (quarterly or annually) on your investments, including any NAV changes and company developments. These updates might include information about new funding rounds, significant business milestones, changes in company leadership, or market conditions affecting the company's sector. Staying informed about these developments helps you understand how your investment is progressing, though remember that pre-IPO investing is a long-term strategy—short-term fluctuations are less important than long-term trajectory.

Tax Considerations and K-1 Forms

Pre-IPO investments in SPVs structured as LLCs involve different tax reporting than public stock investments. Understanding these requirements helps you avoid surprises at tax time.

What is a K-1?: When you invest in an SPV structured as a partnership or LLC (which is typical for pre-IPO funds), you'll receive a Schedule K-1 tax form each year rather than a 1099 form. The K-1 reports your proportional share of the SPV's income, deductions, and credits for the tax year. Even if the SPV doesn't distribute any cash to you during the year, you may owe taxes on your share of the SPV's income.

K-1 Timing: K-1 forms are typically distributed later than 1099 forms—often in March or even April—because the SPV must complete its own tax reporting before generating K-1s for investors. If you typically file your taxes early, you'll need to wait for your K-1s before filing, or file an extension.

What K-1s Include: Your K-1 will report various items of income, deductions, and credits allocated to you based on your ownership percentage. For most pre-IPO SPVs that simply hold shares without generating income, K-1s may show minimal activity until an exit event. However, if the underlying company pays dividends or the SPV incurs expenses, these will flow through to your K-1.

Working with Tax Professionals: Many investors find it helpful to work with accountants or tax professionals familiar with partnership taxation when dealing with K-1s, especially if they hold multiple SPV investments. The tax treatment can be complex, and professional guidance ensures you're reporting correctly and taking advantage of any available deductions or credits.

Secondary Trading Opportunities

While pre-IPO investments are generally illiquid with expected holding periods of 2-10 years, some platforms offer secondary trading opportunities through Alternative Trading Systems (ATS). For most SPVs, secondary trading can begin on an ATS after an initial 1 year hold period.

What is an ATS?: An Alternative Trading System is a regulated platform that facilitates trading of securities outside traditional stock exchanges. For pre-IPO shares, an ATS allows investors to potentially sell their SPV membership interests or underlying company shares to other accredited investors before the company goes public.

How Secondary Trading Works: If you need liquidity before an IPO or acquisition, you may be able to list your investment for sale on an ATS. Other investors can view available offerings and potentially purchase your position. However, several important caveats apply:

No Guarantee of Sale: Unlike public stocks that have continuous liquidity, private shares may not have buyers at any given time. You might need to wait for interested parties or price your offering attractively to generate interest.

Company Approval May Be Required: Many private companies maintain transfer restrictions requiring them to approve any share transfers, even on secondary markets. This approval process can affect timing and certainty.

Pricing Considerations: You may need to price your offering at a discount to current estimated value to attract buyers, especially if you're seeking quick liquidity.

SPV-Specific Rules: Some SPVs have their own rules, fees, and processes. Familiarize yourself with these before attempting to sell. These will be made clear and accessible prior to any secondary trading activity in the SPV commencing.

Secondary trading can provide a valuable liquidity option if your circumstances change, but it's best to invest in pre-IPO opportunities with the expectation that you'll hold until a formal exit event.

Corporate Actions and Their Impact

During your holding period, the underlying company may undergo various corporate actions that affect your investment. Understanding these scenarios helps you know what to expect:

Acquisition: If the company gets acquired for cash, this represents a liquidity event for investors. The SPV will receive cash proceeds for its shares based on the acquisition price and terms. After satisfying any liquidation preferences senior to your share class, the SPV will distribute remaining proceeds to investors proportionally. You'll receive your pro-rata share of the distribution, which represents the return on your investment. The distribution timing depends on the acquisition closing timeline, but you can typically expect to receive funds within weeks of the transaction closing.

Merger or Stock Split: If the company merges with another company or undergoes a stock split, your equity stake will adjust to reflect the change in capital structure, similar to how these events work in public markets. For example, in a 2-for-1 stock split, the SPV's 1,000 shares would become 2,000 shares, but each share would be worth proportionally less, maintaining the same total value. In a merger, your shares might convert into shares of the combined entity based on the exchange ratio. These corporate actions don't immediately create liquidity but change the structure of your ownership.

Initial Public Offering (IPO): This is often the most anticipated exit scenario for pre-IPO investors. When the company goes public, the SPV's shares will typically convert into publicly traded shares at the IPO price. Depending on the SPV structure and any lockup agreements, you may receive your shares directly (becoming a shareholder of the now-public company) or the SPV may liquidate the position and distribute cash proceeds. Many IPOs include lockup periods (often 180 days) during which pre-IPO shareholders cannot sell their shares, designed to prevent excessive selling pressure on the newly public stock. After the lockup expires, you can sell your shares on public markets if you choose, or continue holding for potential additional appreciation.

Follow-On Funding Rounds: If the company raises additional capital through new funding rounds, this typically doesn't require any action from you, but it will affect your investment. New funding rounds can be positive (indicating growth and investor demand) or dilutive (reducing your percentage ownership if new shares are issued). The impact on your investment value depends on the funding round's valuation—if it occurs at a higher valuation than your investment price, it's typically positive; if it's a "down round" at a lower valuation, it may negatively impact your position.

Bankruptcy or Restructuring: As with public company investments, if the underlying company faces financial distress, your investment may lose significant value or become worthless. The outcome depends on the specific situation and your share class's position in the capital structure. Preferred shares with liquidation preferences have priority over common shares in bankruptcy scenarios. This is one reason why thorough due diligence and portfolio diversification are essential in pre-IPO investing—not every company will succeed, and being prepared for this possibility helps you manage risk appropriately.

Staying Connected

Throughout your holding period, platforms typically provide updates on your investments through several channels:

Email notifications: Important company developments, funding rounds, or platform updates

Portfolio dashboard: Access to current NAV estimates, transaction history, and documents

Annual reports: Summary of SPV performance and any distributions

Company updates: Some platforms share relevant news articles or company announcements

Staying engaged with these communications helps you remain informed about your investments without requiring active management. Pre-IPO investing is largely a passive, long-term strategy, but informed passive investing is more effective than blind hope.

Pre-IPO investments represent a unique asset class with their own rhythms and requirements. By understanding how to monitor your investments through NAV, managing your tax obligations with K-1 forms, recognizing potential secondary trading opportunities, and being prepared for various corporate action scenarios, you can navigate the post-investment period confidently. While these investments require patience and a longer-term perspective than public stocks, the potential for enhanced returns and access to innovative companies makes them a compelling component of a diversified investment portfolio for those willing to embrace their unique characteristics.
